# Proposal Architecture System — Autoflow Solutions

## Overview

### Proposal Philosophy

A proposal is not a quote sheet. It is not a PDF attachment sent into the void hoping someone reads it before the weekend. It is a persuasion document — a structured argument that proves you understand the buyer's problem better than they do, and that Autoflow Solutions is the only logical answer.

Every section of the proposal earns its place by doing one of three things:

1. Reinforcing the buyer's pain so they feel understood
2. Building the case that inaction is more expensive than action
3. Connecting Autoflow's specific capabilities to the specific outcome they said they wanted

If a section does none of these three things, it does not belong in the proposal.

The goal is not to impress. The goal is to close. A proposal that earns a "yes" on a call is better than a 40-page document that earns a "we'll think about it."

---

### When to Send a Proposal

The proposal is only sent after the MEDDPICC score reaches 50 or higher. No exceptions.

A MEDDPICC score below 50 means one or more of the following is true:
- You do not know who makes the decision
- You have not confirmed that budget exists
- You have not mapped the economic impact of the problem
- The prospect has not shown a credible reason to act now

Sending a proposal to a prospect who scores below 50 wastes the proposal. It also trains the prospect to expect detailed work for free and say nothing in return.

If the score is below 50, your next step is another discovery call, not a proposal. Go back and fill the gaps.

**MEDDPICC minimum thresholds before proposal:**
- Metrics: score of 1 or higher (you know what success looks like in numbers)
- Economic Buyer: identified and spoken to, or meeting confirmed
- Decision Criteria: you know at least two factors they are evaluating
- Pain: confirmed, specific, and in their words
- Champion: someone inside the business is actively helping you move forward

If any of those five are missing, the proposal waits.

---

### Proposal Delivery Method

Proposals are never sent cold. A proposal sent without a call is a price sheet. The prospect reads the number, makes a gut decision with no context, and you have no ability to address objections in real time.

The standard delivery process is:

1. Schedule a "proposal review call" — not a "pitch call," not a "presentation" — a review call. Frame it as walking through what you found together.
2. Share your screen during the call and navigate the proposal in real time.
3. Do not send the proposal document before the call. Send it during the call, or after, as a summary.
4. Close on the call, or book the decision call within 48 hours.

If the prospect asks you to "just send it over," you say:

> "I want to make sure we walk through it together so I can answer questions in the moment — the proposal only makes sense with context. Can we grab 30 minutes?"

If they still push back, send a teaser executive summary only and book the full walkthrough.

## Win Theme Development

A win theme is not a tagline. It is a single, specific argument that connects the buyer's most urgent pain to Autoflow's most relevant advantage — stated in terms of the buyer's world, not Autoflow's product catalog.

Every proposal is built around one win theme. That theme appears in the executive summary, the solution section, and the proof section. If it is not threaded through all three, the proposal feels generic.

---

### Three Win Theme Scenarios

---

**Win Theme:**
> "Every inquiry you miss after 5pm is a competitor's new customer."

**Buyer Need:**
The prospect is losing inbound leads outside business hours or during peak volume. They are manually handling inquiry follow-up, which is slow or inconsistent. They know they are missing revenue but have not quantified it. Their emotional state is frustration mixed with resignation — they have accepted the leak as a cost of doing business.

**Differentiator:**
Autoflow's Engagement Engine runs 24/7, responds to leads within seconds, qualifies them automatically, and books them into the calendar without human intervention. Unlike a chatbot, it is trained on the business's specific services and handles objections before a human ever gets involved.

**Proof Point:**
Le Gourmandin (Malaysia): After deploying the Engagement Engine for online booking and FAQ handling, no-show rates dropped 40%. The business recovered an estimated [X] hours per week previously spent on manual confirmation calls.

**Where the Win Theme Appears:**
- Executive Summary: sentence two (the cost of inaction)
- Understanding Your Challenge: the "what is currently happening at 5:01pm" framing
- Our Recommended Solution: first paragraph of the Engagement Engine description
- Proof: Le Gourmandin case study block
- Why Autoflow: the "built for businesses that cannot afford to miss a lead" argument

---

#### Win Theme Scenario B — Track B: Admin Overhead Prospect

**Win Theme:**
> "Your team is doing $15/hr work with $60/hr people."

**Buyer Need:**
The prospect's staff — operations managers, coordinators, bookkeepers — are spending large portions of their week on repetitive, low-judgment tasks: data entry, report pulling, scheduling, invoice processing, compliance documentation. The business owner feels this instinctively but has not measured it. They are either thinking about hiring another admin or already have one who is still overwhelmed.

**Differentiator:**
Autoflow's Operations Engine and Execution Engine automate the recurring workflow layer of the business. The system handles the tasks that require consistency and volume, not judgment. Staff are redirected to work that actually requires their skills. The business does not need to hire to grow throughput.

**Proof Point:**

**Where the Win Theme Appears:**
- Executive Summary: the tension statement (your highest-cost people are doing your lowest-value work)
- Understanding Your Challenge: the time audit framing (how many hours per week, which tasks)
- Our Recommended Solution: Operations Engine and/or Execution Engine recommendation
- Proof: We Patrol and Studio Giovanni Carnevale case study blocks
- Why Autoflow: the ROI guarantee framing

#### Win Theme Scenario C — Track C: Phone Volume Prospect

**Win Theme:**
> "Your front desk is a bottleneck. Your AI doesn't have to be."

**Buyer Need:**
The prospect is fielding a high volume of inbound calls — bookings, FAQ questions, status checks, cancellations. The front desk is overwhelmed. Calls go unanswered. Staff are interrupted constantly. Unlike a phone tree or IVR, the Voice Engine holds a real conversation. It does not require the caller to press 1 for anything.

**Proof Point:**

**Where the Win Theme Appears:**
- Executive Summary: the bottleneck framing (your front desk is the constraint)
- Understanding Your Challenge: the call volume and interruption pattern
- Our Recommended Solution: Voice Engine recommendation and call type breakdown
- Proof: Voice Engine benchmark block
- Why Autoflow: the "your team picks up only what matters" argument

## Executive Summary Templates

The executive summary is the most important page in the proposal. It is the only section a time-pressed decision-maker will read before deciding whether to keep reading. It must earn the rest of the document.

Structure:
1. Mirror the buyer's situation (their words, their numbers)
2. Central tension (what it costs them to stay where they are)
3. Solution thesis (the win theme, stated plainly)
4. Proof (one concrete data point from a comparable business)
5. Transformed state (what their business looks like after Autoflow)

Keep each executive summary under 250 words. No filler. No feature lists.

## Full Proposal Structure

The proposal follows a nine-section architecture. Each section has a specific job. Do not add sections because they look professional. Do not remove sections because they feel uncomfortable. The order is deliberate — it mirrors the psychology of a good sales conversation.

---

### Section 1: Cover / Title Page

**What to include:**
- Client business name (large, prominent)
- "Prepared for: [BUYER NAME], [TITLE]"
- "Prepared by: [YOUR NAME], Autoflow Solutions"
- Date (the actual call date, not the send date — it signals responsiveness)
- Proposal reference number (optional, adds professionalism for larger clients)
- Autoflow Solutions logo and contact info

**What not to include:**
- A generic tagline
- A photo of software dashboards
- Any pricing on the cover
- The word "confidential" (it is implied and makes small proposals look performative)

**Design note:** The cover should feel clean and direct. The client's name should be more prominent than Autoflow's name. This signals that the proposal is about them, not about you.

---

### Section 2: Executive Summary

Use the template that matches the buyer's pain track (A, B, or C). Fill in every bracketed field using the exact language from the Friction Audit call notes. Do not paraphrase. If the buyer said "we're drowning in admin," that phrase appears in the executive summary verbatim.

**Job of this section:** Make the buyer feel seen in the first 60 seconds of reading.

---

### Section 3: Understanding Your Challenge

**Job of this section:** Demonstrate that you listened. Prove that you understand the business before you start talking about the solution.

**Structure:**

**3.1 — Your Business Context**
Two to three sentences about what the business does, who they serve, and where they are in their growth trajectory. Write this from memory, not from their website. If you cannot do that, you did not do enough discovery.

Example:
> "[CLIENT BUSINESS NAME] operates [X locations / a team of X] serving [customer type] in [geography]. Over the last [timeframe], you have [grown / scaled / expanded] and the operational complexity has grown with it — without a commensurate increase in systems to manage that complexity."

**3.2 — The Problem as You Understand It**
Restate the problem in precise terms. Use numbers where they have them. Use ranges where they do not. Avoid vague language.

Fill-in template:
> "Based on our conversation on [DATE], the core challenge is [the specific problem in their words]. This manifests as:
>
> - [Specific symptom 1 — e.g., 'Inquiry response time averaging 4-6 hours after business hours']
> - [Specific symptom 2 — e.g., 'Front desk staff spending approximately 3 hours per day on manual data entry']
> - [Specific symptom 3 — e.g., '15-20 calls per day going unanswered or to voicemail']
>
> The downstream effect of this is [the business impact — revenue loss, staff frustration, customer experience degradation]."

**3.3 — What This Costs You**
This is the cost-of-inaction framing. Be specific. Use the numbers they gave you during discovery.

Fill-in template:
> "At your current volume, this problem costs approximately:
>
> - [X] hours per week in [manual task / staff time / follow-up]
> - [$Y] per month in [lost leads / unnecessary headcount / overtime / rework]
> - [Z] opportunities per month that go unanswered or fall through the cracks
>
> Over 12 months, that is [$XX,XXX] in friction your business is absorbing and passing on."

**Tone note:** Do not editorialize here. Do not say "shockingly" or "unfortunately." State the numbers and let the math land on its own.

---

### Section 4: Our Recommended Solution

**Job of this section:** Present the engine recommendation with specificity, not generality. The buyer should understand exactly what they are buying and why it was chosen for their situation.

**Structure:**

**4.1 — What We Recommend and Why**
Name the engine(s). Explain the selection logic based on their pain track.

Fill-in template:
> "Based on what you described, we recommend the [ENGINE NAME] — specifically because [the reason connected to their pain track, not a generic feature list].
>
> For [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME], this means [the specific capability most relevant to their situation — e.g., 'a booking assistant that handles inquiries on your website, qualifies the lead, and drops them into your calendar without your staff touching it']."

**4.2 — How It Works (Plain Language)**
Three to five bullet points. No technical jargon. No acronyms. Write it as if you are explaining it to a smart person who has never heard of AI agents.

Example for Engagement Engine:
> - Responds to every inquiry on your website within 60 seconds, 24/7
> - Asks qualifying questions based on your intake criteria and captures the answers
> - Books the appointment directly into your calendar system
> - Sends confirmation and reminder messages automatically
> - Escalates to a human only when the inquiry falls outside defined parameters

**4.3 — What It Does Not Do (Objection Pre-Emption 1 — "Is This Just a Chatbot?")**
This is where you address the chatbot objection before the buyer raises it. Embed it naturally into the solution description.

Fill-in template:
> "This is not a scripted chatbot. It does not force your customers through a decision tree or make them press a button to get a response. The [ENGINE NAME] holds a real conversation, handles variation in how questions are asked, and escalates to a human when the situation requires judgment that the system is not designed to handle. The boundary between what it handles and what it escalates is defined during setup and adjusted over time as we learn what your customers actually ask."

**4.4 — What It Does Not Replace**
Briefly acknowledge what the system is not designed to do. This builds trust and sets accurate expectations.

Fill-in template:
> "The [ENGINE NAME] is not a replacement for your [team / front desk / operations manager]. It handles the volume and repetition so they can focus on the work that requires their expertise. Your team remains in the loop for [the specific high-judgment tasks relevant to their business]."

---

### Section 5: Implementation Timeline

**Job of this section:** Remove the ambiguity of "what happens next." Show the buyer exactly what the first 30 days look like so the transition feels manageable.

**Standard four-week rollout:**

**Week 1 — Discovery and Build**
- Kickoff call (60 minutes): we document your workflows, intake criteria, common questions, escalation rules, and calendar logic
- We build the first version of the agent based on your business specifics
- You review and give feedback — no technical skills required

**Week 2 — Integration and Testing**
- We connect the agent to your existing tools (calendar, CRM, communication channels)
- Internal testing: we run simulated conversations to verify the agent handles your most common scenarios correctly
- You test it yourself with real questions and flag anything that needs adjusting

**Week 3 — Soft Launch**
- The agent goes live in a limited capacity (e.g., after-hours only, or one channel)
- We monitor live performance in real time
- You see the first real interactions and give feedback

**Week 4 — Full Deployment and Handover**
- Full rollout across all agreed channels
- We walk you through the reporting dashboard so you can see what the agent is handling
- We set up your first monthly review checkpoint

**Addressing the Mistakes Objection (Objection Pre-Emption 2 — "What If the AI Makes Mistakes?")**
Embed this into the implementation section naturally.

Fill-in template:
> "The rollout is staged because we want real-world feedback before full deployment. If the agent responds incorrectly to a question, we catch it during the soft launch window and correct it before it becomes a pattern. The system also has defined escalation rules — any inquiry it is not confident handling goes to a human immediately. You are not replacing your team's judgment. You are automating the volume that does not require it."

### Section 6: Investment

**Job of this section:** Present price as the natural conclusion of the ROI case, not as a number dropped into a void.

**6.1 — The Value Stack (Present Before the Price)**
Before any number appears, restate what the buyer is getting.

Fill-in template:
> "Here is what this engagement includes:
>
> - A custom-built [ENGINE NAME] trained on [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME]'s specific services, intake criteria, and escalation rules
> - Full integration with your existing [calendar / CRM / communication tools]
> - Four-week implementation with hands-on support at every stage
> - Monthly performance review and ongoing optimization
> - Priority support for adjustments as your business evolves
> - [Any additional deliverable specific to their use case]"

**6.2 — The ROI Anchor**
State the cost-of-inaction numbers before presenting the investment.

Fill-in template:
> "Based on what you told us, you are currently losing approximately [X] hours per week to [the specific task] and [Y leads / $Z in avoidable costs] per month. At your current run rate, that is [$XX,XXX] per year in friction.
>
> The investment in the [ENGINE NAME] is a setup fee of [$SETUP] and a monthly retainer of [$MONTHLY]. In month one, you are spending [$TOTAL MONTH 1]. By month three, if we hit the targets we described, you have recovered more than that in [time saved / leads captured / overhead reduced]."

**6.3 — Price Presentation**
State the price clearly. No hedging. No "starting from." No vague ranges.

Fill-in template:
> "The investment for [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME] is:
>
> **Setup fee:** $[SETUP] (one time, covers build, integration, and launch)
> **Monthly retainer:** $[MONTHLY]/month (covers management, optimization, and support)
> **Month one total:** $[SETUP + MONTHLY]
>
> From month two onward, you are at $[MONTHLY]/month."

**6.4 — The Guarantee**
State the ROI guarantee plainly. Do not bury it. It earns its own paragraph.

Fill-in template:
> "If the [ENGINE NAME] does not save [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME] at least 10 hours per week or $2,000 per month in overhead within the first 60 days, we will not charge the retainer until it does. We are not interested in collecting fees for a system that is not producing results."

**6.5 — Three-Option Presentation (Use When Upselling Multiple Engines)**

When the buyer's pain touches more than one area, present three options. This gives the buyer control over the scope decision without requiring them to negotiate.

**Option 1 — Start with One Engine (Lowest Risk, Fastest Time to Value)**
> "We deploy the [PRIMARY ENGINE] first. This addresses [the most urgent pain] immediately and gives you a baseline to measure ROI before committing to additional scope.
> Setup: $[SETUP 1] | Monthly: $[MONTHLY 1]"

**Option 2 — Two-Engine Deployment (More Coverage, Better ROI)**
> "We deploy the [ENGINE 1] and the [ENGINE 2] in parallel. This addresses [PAIN 1] and [PAIN 2] simultaneously and builds a more complete automation layer for the business.
> Setup: $[SETUP 2] | Monthly: $[MONTHLY 2]"

**Option 3 — Full Implementation (Maximum Leverage)**
> "We deploy [ALL RECOMMENDED ENGINES]. This gives [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME] complete coverage across [lead capture / admin / phone volume]. For businesses ready to scale, this is the highest-leverage entry point.
> Setup: $[SETUP 3] | Monthly: $[MONTHLY 3]"

**Guidance note:** Most buyers select Option 2. Option 1 exists to give the risk-averse a starting point. Option 3 exists to make Option 2 feel reasonable. Never present options without being prepared to recommend one of them explicitly if asked.

**Job of this section:** Remove the buyer's doubt by showing that another business with similar characteristics solved a similar problem using Autoflow.

**Selection rule:** Choose the case study that is closest to the buyer's industry, problem, and scale. If you have two case studies that fit, include both. If neither is a perfect match, lead with the one that matches the problem type, not the industry.

Use the case study block templates from the Case Study Blocks section below. Each block should include:
- Business name (or category if unnamed)
- The situation before Autoflow
- What was deployed
- The specific outcome in numbers
- One quote if available

### Section 8: Why Autoflow

**Job of this section:** Make a specific argument for choosing Autoflow Solutions over every other option the buyer is considering. This is not a feature list. It is not a company bio. It is a direct response to the competitive alternatives.

**8.1 — vs. Zapier / Make**
> "Tools like Zapier and Make are connectors. They move data between systems when you tell them exactly what to do. They do not handle conversation. They do not qualify a lead. They do not answer a phone call. What we build is not a workflow. It is an agent — a system that can respond, reason within defined parameters, and take action. If you have tried Zapier and still have this problem, it is because Zapier was not designed for it."

**8.2 — vs. Hiring Staff**
> "Another hire costs $[X]/year in salary, plus benefits, onboarding time, management overhead, and the risk that they leave in six months. The [ENGINE NAME] costs $[MONTHLY]/month, does not call in sick, does not need to be managed day to day, and scales with your volume without a raise conversation. We are not saying never hire. We are saying: stop using headcount to solve a systems problem."

**8.3 — vs. Doing Nothing**
> "The cost of doing nothing is not zero. You have already quantified it: [restate the cost-of-inaction numbers from Section 3.3]. Doing nothing is a decision to keep paying that cost every month. At $[MONTHLY FRICTION COST] per month in lost leads, admin overhead, or missed calls, the question is not whether the system pays for itself. The question is how many months you want to wait."

**Addressing the "What If We Outgrow It?" Objection (Objection Pre-Emption 3)**
Embed this in the Why Autoflow section.

Fill-in template:
> "The systems we build are not static. Every deployment we do is designed to grow with the business. When your volume doubles, the agent handles double the volume. When your services change, we update the training. When you need to add a channel or integrate a new tool, we do that within the existing retainer. You are not buying a fixed system. You are buying an ongoing capability."

**What Autoflow deployed:** Voice Engine — AI voice agent handling inbound calls, managing common call types (bookings, status checks, FAQ, cancellations) without human involvement, routing complex calls with full context.

**Outcome:**
- 80% of inbound calls handled without human involvement
- Staff answer only calls requiring judgment or relationship management
- Zero missed calls during business hours; after-hours calls handled by the same system

**Use when:** The prospect's primary pain is call volume overwhelming their team or going unanswered.

## Objection Pre-Emption

The three most common objections to the Autoflow proposal are predictable. Rather than waiting for the buyer to raise them and then scrambling to address them, the proposal pre-empts each one in a specific section. The buyer reads the answer before they have consciously formed the objection. This reduces friction at the price presentation and close.

---

### Objection 1: "What If the AI Makes Mistakes?"

**Where it appears in the proposal:** Section 5 (Implementation Timeline), Week 2 and Week 3 language.

**The pre-emption:**
The staged rollout is the answer. Week 2 is internal testing. Week 3 is a soft launch with live monitoring. By the time the system is in full deployment, it has already handled real conversations under observation. Mistakes caught in weeks 2 and 3 are corrected before they become visible to the buyer's customers.

Additionally, the escalation rule is stated clearly: when the system is not confident, it escalates to a human. It does not guess. The buyer's customers are never left in a dead end.

**Language to embed:**
> "Every [ENGINE NAME] goes through a two-stage testing process before full deployment. We run internal simulations in week two, soft launch in week three with live monitoring, and only move to full deployment once we have confirmed the system handles your most common scenarios accurately. The escalation rules are defined at setup — when the agent is not confident, it routes to a human immediately."

---

### Objection 2: "What If We Outgrow It?"

**Where it appears in the proposal:** Section 8 (Why Autoflow), subsection on scalability.

**The pre-emption:**
The system grows with the business. Volume increases do not require re-contracting. Service changes are handled within the ongoing retainer. New channel or tool integrations are added as needed. The buyer is not locked into a static build — they are buying a capability that evolves.

**Language to embed:**
> "The [ENGINE NAME] is not a fixed build. As your volume grows, the system handles it. As your services change, we update the training. As your tools evolve, we integrate them. You are not buying a snapshot of your business today. You are buying an automation layer that stays current with where your business is going."

---

### Objection 3: "Is This Just a Chatbot?"

**Where it appears in the proposal:** Section 4 (Our Recommended Solution), subsection 4.3.

**The pre-emption:**
Call out the distinction explicitly and early. The buyer has had experiences with scripted chatbots that frustrated their customers. Acknowledge that reality and draw a clear line between what those tools do and what Autoflow builds.

**Language to embed:**
> "This is not a scripted chatbot with a button tree and a dead end. The [ENGINE NAME] holds a real conversation, handles variation in phrasing, responds to follow-up questions, and makes decisions within its defined parameters. When it reaches the edge of what it is built to handle, it escalates to a human — with the conversation context intact so your team does not start from zero."

## Proposal Call Script

This is the script for the proposal review call. It is not a word-for-word monologue. It is a sequence with specific language for the key moments.

---

### Opening — Recap the Friction Audit (Target: 2 minutes)

Do not open by saying "So, I put together this proposal for you." Open by referencing the conversation you already had.

> "Before we get into the proposal, I want to spend two minutes making sure I captured things correctly from our last conversation. You told me [restate the core problem in their words]. You mentioned that this is costing you approximately [X hours / Y leads / $Z per month]. And you said the biggest frustration is [their specific frustration, verbatim if possible]. Is that still accurate, or has anything changed?"

Wait for their response. If they correct something, note it and incorporate it. If they confirm it, you have re-established the problem before you present the solution.

---

### Executive Summary Walk-Through

> "I want to start with the executive summary — not because it is the most detailed section, but because it captures the whole argument in one page. Let me read it to you and tell me if it resonates."

Read the executive summary out loud. Do not skip through it. Reading it aloud signals that it was written deliberately.

After reading:
> "Does that feel like an accurate picture of the situation?"

If yes: move to the challenge section.
If they add nuance: "Let me note that — and I want to make sure the solution section reflects it."

---

### Pause Before Pricing

After walking through sections 3, 4, and 5, before moving to the investment section, stop and ask:

> "Before I walk through the investment, I want to check in — does this solution feel directionally right? Is the [ENGINE NAME] the right answer to what you are trying to solve?"

This question serves two purposes:
1. It gets a commitment to the solution before the price creates resistance.
2. It surfaces any unspoken doubt about the fit before the price becomes the blocker.

If they say yes: move to pricing.
If they have a concern: address it before presenting the price. Never present price to a buyer who has an unresolved doubt about the solution.

---

### Price Presentation Script

> "Okay. Based on what we have scoped out, here is the investment.
>
> The setup fee is $[SETUP]. That covers the build, the integration, and the four-week launch process we walked through.
>
> The monthly retainer is $[MONTHLY]. That covers ongoing management, optimization, and support — so the system keeps getting better as we learn what your customers are actually asking.
>
> Month one, you are all in at $[SETUP + MONTHLY]. From month two, you are at $[MONTHLY]/month.
>
> Based on what you told us, you are currently losing approximately [X] hours per week and [$Y] per month in [the specific cost category]. So this pays for itself within [timeframe].
>
> And the guarantee stands: if this does not save [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME] at least 10 hours per week or $2,000 per month within the first 60 days, we do not charge the retainer until it does."

Then stop talking. Do not fill the silence.

---

### Handling Silence After the Price

The most common mistake at this moment is talking through the silence. Do not do it.

After presenting the price, wait. Count to ten internally if you have to. The buyer is processing. Talking through that processing interrupts it and signals that you are nervous about the number.

If the silence extends past 15-20 seconds, ask a neutral question:

> "What's your reaction?"

Not "Is that okay?" Not "Does that work for your budget?" Not "I know it sounds like a lot." Just: "What's your reaction?"

Listen to the full answer before responding.

---

### Close Question

After addressing any questions or concerns:

> "What questions do you have about moving forward?"

This is not "Are you ready to move forward?" That question invites a yes/no and puts the buyer in a defensive position. "What questions do you have about moving forward?" presupposes that moving forward is the next step and asks for the remaining blockers.

If they have no questions:
> "Then the next step is [specific action]. I can send you the agreement now and we can get the kickoff scheduled this week. Does that work?"

---

### Next Step Confirmation

Before ending the call, confirm the specific next step with a date attached.

> "So what I am hearing is [summary of their decision or next step]. I will [send the agreement / send the follow-up email / book the follow-up call] today. You will [sign / confirm / think through the options] by [specific date]. Is that right?"

Do not leave the call without a confirmed next action and a date. "I'll think about it" is not a next step. "I'll think about it and we'll talk again on Thursday at 2pm" is a next step.

## Follow-Up Email Template

Send this email within 30 minutes of ending the proposal call if the buyer did not close on the call. Speed signals professionalism. Sending it three days later signals that you are not organized.

---

### Subject Line Options

Use one of the following. Choose based on the tone of the call:

**Option 1 (Direct):**
`Autoflow + [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME] — Proposal Summary and Next Steps`

**Option 2 (ROI-forward):**
`Your [ENGINE NAME] proposal — the numbers and what happens next`

**Option 3 (Momentum-focused):**
`Following up from today's call — [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME] + Autoflow`

### Email Body (Under 150 Words)

>

>

>
> Next step: [the specific action you agreed on — e.g., "review the agreement and let me know your questions by Thursday"].
>
> If it is easier to talk through anything by phone, I am available [two specific time options].
>
> [YOUR NAME]
> Autoflow Solutions

---

### What to Attach and What Not to Attach

**Attach:**
- The full proposal (PDF format, client-named file: "[ClientBusinessName]_Autoflow_Proposal_[Date].pdf")
- The service agreement (if they are close to signing)

**Do not attach:**
- Generic brochures or product one-pagers
- Case study PDFs (the ones in the proposal are sufficient — additional attachments create noise)
- Pricing tiers or comparison tables not already in the proposal (this creates confusion, not clarity)

---

### Follow-Up Cadence

If there is no response after the follow-up email:

**Day 1 (same day as call):** Send the follow-up email above.

**Day 3:** Brief check-in.
> "Hi [FIRST NAME] — wanted to check in and see if you had a chance to look at the proposal. Happy to jump on a quick call if any questions came up. [YOUR NAME]"

**Day 7:** Final follow-up.
> "Hi [FIRST NAME] — following up one last time on the Autoflow proposal. If the timing is not right, no problem — just let me know and I will close the file for now. If you are still evaluating, I am happy to answer any outstanding questions. [YOUR NAME]"

After day 7, if there is still no response, mark the opportunity as stalled in the CRM and set a re-engage reminder for 30 days. Do not continue following up after three attempts. It signals low value and trains the prospect to ignore your messages.

## Proposal Quality Checklist

Run through this checklist before sending any proposal. If any item is not checked, fix it before the call — not after.

---

### Content Checks

- [ ] The win theme is stated explicitly in the executive summary
- [ ] The win theme is reinforced in the solution section (Section 4)
- [ ] The win theme is connected to a proof point in the case study section (Section 7)
- [ ] The buyer's specific language from the Friction Audit is used verbatim at least once (search the proposal for their exact phrase and confirm it appears)
- [ ] Every numeric claim has a source (either the buyer's own numbers from discovery, or a named case study)
- [ ] No claims are made without evidence (no "typically," "usually," or "many businesses find" without a specific example)
- [ ] The cost-of-inaction estimate in Section 3.3 uses numbers the buyer gave you, not estimates you invented
- [ ] The ROI anchor in Section 6.2 references the specific hours/month/leads they mentioned in discovery

---

### Structure Checks

- [ ] Pricing does not appear before Section 6
- [ ] The guarantee appears in Section 6.4, after the price is presented
- [ ] The three objection pre-emptions are embedded in their designated sections (Section 4.3, Section 5, Section 8)
- [ ] The next steps in Section 9 are named, dated, and assigned to a specific person
- [ ] The case study selected matches the buyer's pain track (A, B, or C) and is as close to their industry as available

---

### Tone and Language Checks

- [ ] No em dashes anywhere in the document
- [ ] No use of "seamless," "revolutionary," or "game-changing"
- [ ] No vague promises (if you cannot put a number on it, rephrase or cut it)
- [ ] The executive summary is under 250 words
- [ ] The follow-up email is under 150 words
- [ ] Every bracketed field [ ] has been filled in (search for "[" and confirm zero remaining instances)

---

### Delivery Checks

- [ ] A proposal review call is scheduled — do not send the full proposal without a call booked
- [ ] The proposal is saved as a PDF with the naming convention: [ClientBusinessName]_Autoflow_Proposal_[Date].pdf
- [ ] The proposal is personalized with the client's business name on the cover and in the document
- [ ] You have your notes from the Friction Audit open during the call so you can reference specific details they mentioned

---

### After the Call

- [ ] Follow-up email sent within 30 minutes of the call ending
- [ ] CRM updated with call notes, decision status, and agreed next step
- [ ] Next follow-up date entered in CRM (Day 3 or Day 7 depending on status)
- [ ] If they did not close: calendar invite sent for next touchpoint with agenda

- [CLIENT BUSINESS NAME]
- [BUYER NAME]
- [TITLE]
- [DATE] (proposal date)
- [YOUR NAME]

- [Specific outcome] — the measurable result the buyer will see (derived from discovery)

- [DATE — 48-72 hours after the proposal call] — agreement deadline
- [DATE — within one week of agreement] — kickoff call target
- [DATE — Week 1 end] — build completion target
