# Friction Audit Discovery Playbook — Autoflow Solutions

**For internal use | Sales team and AI discovery coach agent**

---

## Overview

### Purpose of the Friction Audit

The Friction Audit is not a sales call. It is a structured 30-minute diagnostic conversation. Your job on this call is to act like an operations consultant who has seen the same problem fifty times and is now figuring out whether this specific business has the same root cause.

The name matters. You are auditing their friction. You are not pitching software. You are not demoing a product. You are mapping where time, money, and leads are leaking out of their business, and you are doing it in a way that lets them feel that insight for themselves.

The philosophy is operator-to-operator. The prospect is a business owner or ops manager. They have heard vendor pitches before. They do not need another one. What they have probably never had is a 30-minute session where someone systematically walked through their daily workflow and put a dollar figure on the waste.

That is the value of this call, regardless of whether they buy anything.

### Key Metrics to Hit

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Show rate (booked to attended) | 70-85% |
| Conversion to proposal (attended to proposal sent) | 50-70% |
| Proposal to close | 40-60% |
| Average time from Friction Audit to signed proposal | 3-7 days |

If your show rate is below 70%, the problem is in your pre-call sequence, not the call itself. See the follow-up section for reminder cadence.

If your conversion to proposal is below 50%, you are either booking unqualified prospects or failing to quantify the problem cost during the call.

### What Success Looks Like at the End of This Call

A successful Friction Audit ends with three things confirmed:

1. **The problem is real and quantified.** You and the prospect have agreed on a number: hours per week wasted, leads lost per month, or calls going unanswered. The number is in their words, not yours.

2. **The fit is clear.** You know which engine solves their problem (Engagement, Operations, or Voice) and you have briefly explained why. They understand the connection between their pain and the solution.

3. **There is a defined next step.** Either a proposal call is booked before you hang up, or there is a specific follow-up action with a specific date. "I'll think about it" is not a next step.

## Pre-Call Preparation (15 Minutes Before)

### Research Checklist

Do this in the 15 minutes before the call, not the day before. You want it fresh.

**LinkedIn (5 minutes)**
- [ ] How long have they been in this role? New owners are often still building processes and more open to change. Established owners have more ingrained habits.
- [ ] Recent posts or activity: Are they talking about hiring, being busy, growth, or stress? This is context for the conversation.
- [ ] Company page: How many employees listed? Multiple locations?
- [ ] Any mutual connections you can reference?

**Website (3 minutes)**
- [ ] Do they have a contact form, chat widget, or booking button? If they have none of these, after-hours lead loss is almost certain.
- [ ] Are they advertising services actively? If yes, they care about conversion.
- [ ] What is the main service? Understand it well enough to speak their language.

**Pain Signal That Triggered Outreach (2 minutes)**
- [ ] Review your original outreach. What signal did you use? Hiring post, Google Ads, review volume, referral?
- [ ] Pull up the specific signal so you can reference it on the call: "I noticed you posted about hiring a receptionist last month..."

**CRM Notes (2 minutes)**
- [ ] Read any prior touchpoints. Did they click a link, fill out the AI Self Audit, or use the ROI Calculator?
- [ ] If they completed the ROI Calculator, pull that data. It is the best opening you can have.
- [ ] Note which pain track they came through (A, B, or C) based on the entry point.

### What to Confirm Before Dialing

| Item | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Tool stack (if known) | Helps you understand integration complexity and position fit |
| Team size | Determines scope of automation opportunity |
| Industry | Lets you reference relevant proof points during the call |
| Pain track hypothesis | You should enter the call with a hypothesis. Be ready to be wrong. |

### Mental Preparation

Before you dial, repeat this to yourself: your job is to audit, not to sell.

If you go into this call trying to close, you will talk too much. You will pitch before you understand. You will miss the signal. The prospect will feel sold to, and they will disengage.

Your job is to ask sharp questions, listen hard, reflect back what you hear, and quantify the cost of their problem. If the fit is real, the recommendation will feel obvious to both of you by minute 22. It will not feel like a pitch. It will feel like the logical conclusion of an honest conversation.

If the fit is not real, end the call early and say so. That builds more trust than pushing a bad fit.

## Upfront Contract (Call Opening — First 2 Minutes)

### Why the Upfront Contract Matters

The upfront contract kills ambiguity. It tells the prospect exactly what is going to happen, how long it will take, and what both parties will get out of it. It gives you permission to ask hard questions. It prevents the call from drifting into small talk that never gets anywhere.

Without an upfront contract, prospects often show up guarded and you spend the first ten minutes doing pleasantries before you earn the right to ask anything meaningful.

### Word-for-Word Opening Script

Use this verbatim until it feels natural. Then adapt it to your voice, but keep every structural element.

---

"Hey [Name], thanks for making the time. Really appreciate it.

So just so we're on the same page about how this works: we've got about 30 minutes today. The way I like to run these is pretty straightforward.

For the first half, I'm going to ask you some questions about how your business runs right now. Specifically the parts that feel like friction: where time is getting lost, where leads might be falling through the cracks, where your team is doing things manually that probably don't need a human. I want to understand your current state before I say anything about what we do.

For the second half, if it looks like there's a fit, I'll walk you through what we'd actually build and why, based on what you've told me. If there's no fit, I'll tell you that too.

At the end, one of two things will happen: either we'll agree there's something worth exploring and book 20 minutes to walk through a specific proposal, or we'll shake hands and you'll have a clearer picture of what to fix in your operations even if it's not with us.

Does that work for you?

And just so I can make the most of your time: from everything I know about your business so far, the area that looked like the biggest leak to me was [after-hours lead response / admin overhead / inbound phone volume]. Is that the right place to start, or is there something else you'd want me to look at first?"

---

### Notes on the Opening

- The phrase "shake hands and you'll have a clearer picture" is important. It positions the call as valuable even if they do not buy. This reduces defensiveness.
- Naming the pain area at the end of the opening does two things: it shows you did your homework, and it gives them the chance to correct you if you are wrong. Either way, you are starting with a specific problem, not a generic conversation.
- If they say "actually the bigger issue is X," treat that as a gift. Pivot immediately.
- Do not rush this. Two minutes is the right length for the opening. If you compress it, you lose the permission structure.

## Pain Track Qualification (Minutes 2-8)

### Identifying the Right Pain Track

Every prospect falls into one of three tracks. Your job in minutes 2-8 is to confirm which one and go deep on it. Do not try to cover all three. Pick the right one and stay there.

| Track | Core Pain | Entry Product |
|---|---|---|
| A | After-hours lead loss, slow response, missed inquiries | Engagement Engine ($150/mo + $899 setup) |
| B | Admin overhead, manual tasks, data entry, CRM chaos | Operations Engine ($170/mo + $1,699 setup) |
| C | Inbound call volume, missed calls, manual booking over phone | Voice Engine ($200/mo + $1,999 setup) |

You usually enter the call with a hypothesis based on the pain signal. Confirm it in the first question.

### Diagnostic Questions by Track

#### Track A: After-Hours Lead Loss

Use these questions when the prospect is a service business that runs on inquiries or bookings, and where response time or availability is a known issue.

1. "When someone reaches out after hours, what typically happens? Walk me through it from their side."
2. "How many new inquiries do you get in a typical week? And of those, how many would you say convert to a booking or a client?"
3. "What's your typical response time when a new lead comes in? And is that consistent across weekends and evenings?"
4. "Do you have any sense of how many people reach out and then just... go quiet? They contact you but you never hear back?"
5. "If someone fills out your contact form or sends a DM at 9pm on a Friday, what does their experience look like until Monday morning?"
6. "Have you ever looked at your missed call log or contact form submissions and thought about what percentage of those actually turned into revenue?"

#### Track B: Admin Overhead

Use these questions when the prospect has a team doing repetitive manual tasks: data entry, chasing documents, sending follow-ups, updating spreadsheets, copying information between systems.

1. "If you had to pick the one task your team does every week that probably shouldn't need a human, what would it be?"
2. "Walk me through what happens when a new client comes on board. Step by step, what does your team have to do manually?"
3. "How much time per week do you think gets spent on tasks like data entry, copying information between systems, or sending the same types of emails?"
4. "When something falls through the cracks, what is it usually? A follow-up that didn't get sent? An invoice that was late? A document that wasn't chased?"
5. "Are your team members doing work that you'd describe as below their pay grade? Stuff that an assistant should be doing but there isn't one?"
6. "If I told you that the average operations-heavy SME spends 15-25 hours a week on tasks that could be 80% automated, does that feel like an overestimate for your business or an underestimate?"

#### Track C: Phone Volume

Use these questions when the prospect is in a high-inbound-call business: clinics, service businesses, salons, home services, property, hospitality.

1. "How many inbound calls does your business take on a typical day? And what percentage of those are booking requests versus questions you get over and over?"
2. "What happens when all your lines are busy or your team is with a client and a call comes in?"
3. "Do you have visibility into how many calls go unanswered in a week? Or do you just not know?"
4. "Of the calls you do answer, how many are for things that really could be handled without a human? Appointment confirmations, hours, pricing, directions?"
5. "Is phone handling something your team finds draining? Do they feel like it interrupts actual work?"
6. "Have you ever tried an IVR system or a basic phone bot before? What happened?"

### Listening for the "Aha Signal"

The Aha Signal is the moment when the prospect stops giving you the polished version of their business and starts describing the actual mess. It usually sounds like one of these:

- "Honestly, it's a disaster..."
- "We lose track of it all the time..."
- "I have no idea what the actual number is, but it's a lot..."
- "My team hates it, but we just haven't found a better way..."
- "I've been meaning to fix that for two years..."

When you hear the Aha Signal, slow down. Do not rush to your next question. Reflect it back:

"Say more about that. What does that look like on a typical week?"

Then stay quiet and listen. The Aha Signal is the emotional core of the problem. Everything you say for the rest of the call should connect back to it.

## Current State Mapping (Minutes 8-18)

### The 5-Question Current State Map

Once you know the pain track and you have heard the Aha Signal, your job is to map exactly what happens in their business right now. Step by step. No assumptions.

The goal is to build a mental picture that you can reflect back to them in a way that makes the cost of the problem impossible to ignore.

Ask these five questions in order, adapting the language to their specific situation:

1. **"Walk me through exactly what happens when [the triggering event occurs]."**
   Example: "Walk me through exactly what happens when a new lead fills out your contact form."

2. **"Who is responsible for handling that? Is it always the same person, or does it rotate?"**
   This surfaces single points of failure and human dependency.

3. **"How long does that typically take, per occurrence? And how many times does that happen in a week?"**
   This is where you start calculating.

4. **"What goes wrong most often in this process? What's the thing that breaks or gets dropped?"**
   This surfaces the real cost: lost revenue, frustrated clients, rework.

5. **"If you could wave a wand and that entire process just happened automatically and correctly every time, what would that free up your team to do instead?"**
   This opens the door to vision and ROI framing.

### How to Calculate the Cost of Their Process

Use this formula in your head (or on a notepad) as they are talking:

**Time cost per week = (Minutes per occurrence) x (Occurrences per week) / 60 = Hours per week**

**Dollar cost per week = Hours per week x Effective hourly rate of person doing it**

For most SME owner-operators or admin staff, use $35-50/hour as a conservative blended rate. For professional services (accountants, consultants), use $75-150/hour.

Then multiply by 4 for a monthly cost.

Example:
- Admin spends 12 minutes updating CRM after each new lead
- 20 leads per week
- 12 x 20 = 240 minutes = 4 hours per week
- At $40/hour = $160/week = $640/month
- Plus error cost, delay cost, and opportunity cost

That is before you have said a single word about Autoflow.

### The Reflection Template

After the current state map, reflect back what you heard using this exact structure. Do it before you pitch anything.

"So just to make sure I understand what's happening right now:

[X] happens — let's say it's a new inquiry coming in after hours — and because [Y] — your team isn't available — the prospect waits until the next morning to hear back.

That's a window of, what, 8 to 12 hours? And in that window, you said maybe 30% of them have already booked somewhere else.

If you're getting 40 inquiries a week and losing 12 of them, and each booking is worth [Z], that's roughly [calculated loss] per month walking out the door. Is that a fair picture?"

Wait for them to confirm, correct, or add to it. Never rush past the reflection. This is where they feel understood.

### Track-Specific Question Banks

#### Track A Deep-Dive Questions

- "What does your current lead capture setup look like? Contact form, phone, chat, social DM, all of the above?"
- "Do you have any automation in place right now for following up with new inquiries? Even basic stuff?"
- "What's the longest a lead has waited to hear from you, and do you know what happened to that one?"
- "Have you ever run any kind of re-engagement on cold leads? People who reached out and then went silent?"
- "Is there a script or a standard message your team uses when they respond to new inquiries, or does everyone do it differently?"

#### Track B Deep-Dive Questions

- "What tools are you using right now for your operations? CRM, project management, communication?"
- "When information needs to move from one system to another, who does that? Is it a human copy-pasting, or is there any automation?"
- "How do you handle client onboarding right now? What does the welcome sequence look like?"
- "What's your document management process? Contracts, intake forms, ID checks, anything like that?"
- "If a team member calls in sick tomorrow, what breaks? What task that was in their head is now invisible?"
- "Do you track how long tasks take? Or is that something you've never really measured?"

#### Track C Deep-Dive Questions

- "What are the top three reasons people call you? Is it mostly bookings, questions about services, directions, pricing?"
- "Do you have a voicemail? And if so, do those messages actually get followed up on same day?"
- "How long does a typical booking call take? Start to finish."
- "Have you lost a client or had a complaint specifically because a call wasn't answered?"
- "What's the cost of one missed booking for your business? Just one."
- "If you automated 80% of your inbound calls, what would your team be doing with that time?"

## ROI Quantification Moment (Minutes 18-22)

### The Principle

Never pitch before they have quantified the problem themselves. The ROI Quantification Moment is where you help them do the math out loud, in their own words, before you have said anything about what you sell.

If they calculate their own loss, the solution is not a cost. It is a recovery.

### How to Run the ROI Moment

Step 1: Use the numbers you built during current state mapping.

Step 2: Ask them to confirm or correct the estimate:

"Based on what you've told me, I'm roughly calculating [X hours per week] tied up in [admin task / manual follow-up / phone handling]. At a conservative rate for someone in your position, that's about [dollar amount] per month in pure time cost. Does that feel in the right ballpark to you?"

Step 3: Add the inaction tax.

"And that's just the direct time cost. That doesn't count the leads that didn't convert because of slow follow-up, or the clients who didn't come back because they had a frustrating experience trying to book. That's harder to quantify exactly, but it's real."

Step 4: Let the silence sit. Do not jump in with a solution yet.

Step 5: Then ask: "What would it mean for your business if that cost went away?"

### The Inaction Tax Framing

The inaction tax is the cost of staying exactly where they are for another 12 months.

"If nothing changes, and you're losing [X] per month to this process, that's [$X x 12] over the next year. The question isn't whether you can afford to automate this. The question is whether you can afford not to."

Use this framing only when the number is meaningful. Do not use it if the number is small. The inaction tax argument needs a number worth caring about.

### ROI Guarantee Positioning

Autoflow's guarantee: "If it doesn't save 10 hours per week or $2,000 per month in overhead, we won't build it."

Introduce this after the inaction tax calculation:

"The way we work: before we build anything, we scope it against a specific benchmark. If we can't see a clear path to saving you at least 10 hours a week or $2,000 a month, we tell you that and we don't take the project. So if we move forward, it means we're confident the ROI is there."

This reframes the cost of the engagement as low-risk. They do not have to trust that it will work. You are telling them you will not build it if you do not believe it will.

## Pitch Bridge (Minutes 22-26)

### The Rule

You do not pitch. You recommend, based on what they told you. There is a difference, and the prospect can feel it.

A pitch says: "Here is what we sell."
A recommendation says: "Based on what you've described, here is what I would build for you and why."

### The Bridge Transition Script

"Okay, so here's where my head is after everything you've told me.

You've got [describe their specific problem in their own words]. That's costing you roughly [the number you calculated together]. And right now the only way you're handling it is [their current manual process].

Based on what I'm seeing, the right fit for you is our [Engine name]. Here's specifically what that would mean for your business..."

Then go into the specific engine description. Do not list features. Describe outcomes.

#### Track A Bridge (Engagement Engine)

The setup is $899 and it runs at $150 per month after that. Based on the leads you're describing, you'd recover that setup cost in under a month."

"What you need is a workflow that handles the data movement, the follow-ups, the document chasing, and the CRM updates automatically, without a human having to remember to do it.

Match the proof point to their vertical when possible.

## Objection Handling (AECR Framework)

### The Framework

Every objection is handled with four steps:

- **Acknowledge**: Show that you heard them. Do not argue.
- **Explore**: Ask a question to understand the real concern underneath the stated objection.
- **Counter**: Address the real concern with a specific, factual response.
- **Request**: Ask for what you need next.

Never skip the Explore step. The stated objection is rarely the actual objection.

---

### Objection 1: "We Already Use Zapier / HubSpot"

**Acknowledge:**
"That makes sense. A lot of the businesses we work with had Zapier or HubSpot in place before they came to us."

**Explore:**
"Can I ask: what are you using it for right now? And is it handling the [specific problem you described] end-to-end, or are there still pieces that fall to your team manually?"

**Counter:**
"What we find is that tools like Zapier are great for simple if-then logic, but they break down when the situation needs judgment: qualifying a lead, handling a question that's off-script, managing a multi-step process with exceptions. That's where the AI layer makes a difference. We also build on top of existing tools. We're not asking you to replace HubSpot. We're asking you to make it work without a human in the middle."

**Request:**
"Would it be worth spending 20 minutes mapping out exactly where your current stack leaves gaps? That's basically what the proposal conversation would cover."

---

### Objection 2: "We're Not Ready for AI Yet"

**Acknowledge:**
"That's a fair concern. A lot of business owners feel that way before they see what it actually looks like in practice."

**Explore:**
"What does 'not ready' mean for you specifically? Is it about the technology being too complex, or more about not having the internal process organized enough to hand off to automation?"

**Counter:**
If technology concern: "The systems we build don't require technical knowledge from you or your team. We handle the build, the integration, the testing. You approve the logic. That's it. Most of our clients describe it as surprisingly simple once it's running."

If process concern: "Actually, businesses with messier processes often benefit more. Part of what we do in the build phase is map the process clearly, sometimes for the first time. The automation forces the clarity."

**Request:**
"What would need to be true for you to feel ready? If we could show you what the built version looks like for a business similar to yours, would that help?"

---

### Objection 3: "It's Too Expensive"

**Acknowledge:**
"I hear you. Cost is a real consideration."

**Explore:**
"Can I ask what you're comparing it to? Are you thinking about it in terms of the monthly fee, the setup cost, or total cost over the year?"

**Counter:**
"Let's go back to the numbers we ran. You said [X hours per week] tied up in [process]. That's roughly [Y per month] at your blended rate. The Operations Engine is $170 per month. The math means you're paying for the first month's subscription in the first [X days] of time savings alone. The setup fee is a one-time cost that typically pays back in the first 30-60 days. If the ROI isn't there, we don't build it. That's our actual policy."

**Request:**
"If I put together a specific proposal with the ROI case built in, would that give you enough to make the decision? It won't cost you anything to look at the numbers."

---

### Objection 4: "We Need to Think About It"

**Acknowledge:**
"Totally fair. This is a meaningful decision."

**Explore:**
"What specifically would you be thinking through? Is it budget, timing, or something about the fit that's not clear yet?"

**Counter:**
If budget or timing: "One thing I've found useful is to anchor this to your next 90 days. You described [specific problem]. If you're still running that process manually in 90 days, what does that cost you? That's the comparison point, not the cost of the solution."

If fit is unclear: "That's actually exactly what the proposal call is for. I'm not asking you to decide today. I'm asking you to spend 20 minutes looking at a specific plan scoped to your business. After that, you'll have everything you need to make the decision."

**Request:**
"Can we put 20 minutes on the calendar for [specific date two or three days out]? I'll send the proposal in advance so you can review it before we talk."

---

### Objection 5: "Can You Send Me Some Information?"

**Acknowledge:**
"Happy to send you something."

**Explore:**
"What specifically would be most useful? I want to make sure I'm sending you something relevant to your situation rather than a generic overview."

**Counter:**
"Here's the thing: the most useful thing I can send you is a proposal that's actually scoped to your business, based on what we've talked about today. A general brochure won't tell you what this looks like for a [their industry] with [their team size]. The proposal will."

**Request:**
"Let me send you a short summary of what we covered today and a link to book the proposal call. If after reading it you decide it's not the right time, no problem. But if it resonates, you've got the booking link right there. Does that work?"

Do not send a deck. Send the call summary and the booking link. A deck gives them an excuse to study it indefinitely without ever booking.

## Next Steps Close (Minutes 26-30)

### The Rule

The call does not end without a specific next step. A date, a time, and an action. Not "I'll follow up" and not "let me know when you're ready."

If you hang up without a booked next step, your close rate on this deal drops by more than half.

### Two Paths

#### Path 1: Ready for Proposal

They are engaged, the pain is clear, the fit is confirmed. Close for the proposal call before you hang up.

"Based on everything we've talked about, I think there's a clear fit here. The next step I'd suggest is a 20-minute proposal call where I walk you through exactly what we'd build, the timeline, and the investment. I can usually turn that around in 48 hours.

Does [specific day] work for you, or is [alternative day] better?"

Book it before the call ends. Send the invite while they are still on the line.

#### Path 2: Not Ready Yet

They need more time, or there is a specific barrier to address first. Do not accept "I'll think about it." Get a specific date.

"I understand. What I'll do is send you a summary of what we covered today plus the specific ROI numbers we discussed. Can we put a placeholder on the calendar for [specific date] to reconnect after you've had a chance to look it over? Even if it's just 10 minutes to check in."

If they push back on booking a time: "I get it. I'll send the summary and include a booking link. But just so I know when to follow up: is [specific date] a reasonable time to check back in?"

### Pre-Close on the Proposal Call

Before you hang up, plant this question. It reduces proposal call friction significantly.

"One last thing before we wrap up: when I send over the proposal, what would need to be true for you to move forward? Is it purely the ROI math, or are there other stakeholders involved, or is timing a factor?"

This surfaces hidden objections before the proposal call. If there is a decision-maker you have not met, find out now. If there is a budget approval process, find out now. Do not discover it on the proposal call.

## Common Discovery Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Talking Too Much

If you are talking more than 40% of the time on a discovery call, you are doing it wrong. Your job is to ask, listen, and reflect. Not to explain.

The most common symptom: you get three minutes into your explanation of the Engagement Engine and the prospect says "yeah, we already have something like that." You talked yourself into an objection.

Fix: After every question, wait. Count to three before you say anything. Let them fill the silence.

### Mistake 2: Pitching Before Mapping the Current State

You cannot recommend a solution you do not understand the problem for. If you pitch the Operations Engine at minute 5 because you heard "admin overhead," you will pitch it wrong. You will describe features they do not need and miss the one thing they actually care about.

Fix: Complete the 5-question current state map before you say anything about what you sell. No exceptions.

### Mistake 3: Failing to Quantify the Cost of the Problem

A vague problem is easy to ignore. A $3,200 per month problem is not.

If you leave the current state mapping without a number, you have not done the job. The prospect will not feel urgency. They will not prioritize the follow-up. They will not move the proposal into the yes pile.

Fix: Always run the time-cost calculation during the call. Say the number out loud. Get them to confirm it.

### Mistake 4: Accepting Vague Next Steps

"I'll be in touch" is not a next step. "Let me think about it" is not a next step. "Send me some info" is not a next step.

A vague next step means there is no next step. The deal goes cold.

Fix: End every call with a specific date, a specific time, and a specific action. Book it before you hang up when possible. Use the pre-close question to surface barriers early.

### Mistake 5: Not Using the Upfront Contract

If you skip the upfront contract, the call drifts. The prospect does not know what to expect. They feel ambushed when you start asking hard questions. They feel sold to when you make a recommendation.

The upfront contract gives you permission. It turns the call into a structured conversation rather than a guessing game.

Fix: Use the opening script every time. Adapt the language, but keep the structure: here is what we will do, here is what you will get, here is what I will get, do you agree?

## Call Scoring Rubric

Score your Friction Audit calls out of 10. Review recordings weekly. The goal is to identify patterns, not to punish yourself for a bad call.

### Scoring Criteria

| Category | Points | What to Look For |
|---|---|---|
| Upfront Contract | 1 point | Did you open with a clear agenda, mutual outcome, and get agreement? |
| Pain Track Identification | 1 point | Did you confirm the right pain track within the first 6 minutes? |
| Aha Signal Captured | 1 point | Did the prospect describe the problem in their own emotional language? Did you slow down and explore it? |
| Current State Mapped | 2 points | Did you complete a step-by-step map of their current process? (1 pt) Did you get to a specific time or dollar cost? (1 pt) |
| ROI Quantified | 2 points | Did you calculate the cost out loud with them? (1 pt) Did they confirm the number? (1 pt) |
| Pitch Bridge Used | 1 point | Did you transition to the recommendation using their own words and the AECR framework? |
| Objections Handled | 1 point | If an objection came up, did you use AECR? Did you ask the Explore question before countering? |
| Next Step Secured | 1 point | Did the call end with a specific next step: a booked call, a specific date, or a confirmed proposal send? |

### Score Interpretation

| Score | Interpretation | Priority Action |
|---|---|---|
| 9-10 | Strong call | Review what worked, replicate it |
| 7-8 | Solid with gaps | Identify the one or two categories where points were lost |
| 5-6 | Structurally incomplete | Check if current state was fully mapped and ROI was quantified |
| Below 5 | Rebuild needed | Likely talking too much or skipping the upfront contract |

### How to Review a Recording

Listen to the recording once without taking notes. Then listen again and mark:
- Timestamps where the prospect gave the Aha Signal
- Whether you slowed down at the Aha Signal or rushed past it
- The moment you first mentioned Autoflow's product (should be after minute 18)
- Whether the ROI number was said out loud by the prospect or just by you (it should be both)
- How the call ended: specific next step or vague close?

Review one call per week minimum. Review more when conversion rates are below target.
