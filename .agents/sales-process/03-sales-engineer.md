## Overview

### The Demo's Role in the Autoflow Sales Process

The demo sits between the Friction Audit and the proposal. By the time you get here, you already know:

- Which pain track the prospect is on (A, B, or C)
- Roughly how much time or revenue is leaking each month
- What tools they use
- How they feel about tech (resistant, cautious, or open)

The demo is not a product walkthrough. It is a proof-of-concept moment. You are showing the prospect what their business looks like with the problem solved. The goal is one specific reaction: "I need this."

If the Friction Audit was the diagnosis, the demo is the x-ray. You are not selling software features. You are showing them a version of their operations that does not have the problem they just told you about.

### Demo Philosophy: Show Their World, Not Your Product

Generic demos kill deals. When a prospect sees a demo with a fake business name, irrelevant tools, and example data that has nothing to do with their industry, they mentally check out. They stop seeing the solution and start seeing a pitch.

Before every demo, you must:

1. Rename every workflow, agent, and automation to reflect their actual business
2. Use their tools in the walkthrough (even if simulated)
3. Reference the specific pain they described during the Friction Audit
4. Speak in their language, not automation language

"Workflow trigger" means nothing to a clinic owner. "The moment someone fills out your contact form at 11pm" means everything.

### Three Demo Types Matched to Pain Tracks

| Pain Track | Underlying Problem | Engine to Demo | Primary Signal from Audit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Track A | After-hours lead loss | Engagement Engine | "We miss calls/messages after hours" or "leads go cold overnight" |
| Track B | Admin overhead | Operations Engine | "My team spends hours on admin" or "I'm doing tasks a computer should do" |
| Track C | Phone volume | Voice Engine | "We're always on the phone" or "we miss calls" or "staff spends half their day booking" |

In some cases a prospect will show signals across multiple tracks. Lead with the dominant pain. Mention the secondary capability as an upsell after the close, not during the demo.

## Pre-Demo Checklist (24 Hours Before)

### Confirm from Friction Audit Notes

Before opening your demo environment, pull up your Friction Audit notes and verify:

- [ ] Pain track confirmed (A, B, or C)
- [ ] Primary outcome they care about (leads, time, revenue, or stress)
- [ ] The specific tools they mentioned (CRM, booking system, invoicing, comms)
- [ ] Their typical customer interaction pattern (email, phone, WhatsApp, web form)
- [ ] Any technology resistance or concerns flagged during the audit
- [ ] Decision-maker confirmed as present on the demo call
- [ ] Their business hours and after-hours window (relevant for Track A)
- [ ] Approximate volume (how many leads/calls/tasks per week)

### Which Engine to Demo

Use this as a hard rule: demo only one engine per call unless the prospect explicitly asked about a second one during the audit. Showing two engines in a single demo creates confusion and dilutes the Aha Moment.

- Track A confirmed = Demo Narrative A (Engagement Engine)
- Track B confirmed = Demo Narrative B (Operations Engine)
- Track C confirmed = Demo Narrative C (Voice Engine)
- Mixed signals = ask one clarifying question before the call: "Of the two issues you mentioned, which one is costing you the most right now?"

### Technical Requirements

- Screen share confirmed and tested (Zoom, Google Meet, or Teams depending on their preference)
- Audio quality checked (no echo, no background noise)
- Demo environment pre-loaded and stable
- Backup plan if screen share fails: have a 2-minute recorded walkthrough you can share via link
- If demoing Voice Engine: have a live phone number ready to call or a high-quality audio recording of the AI handling a call
- Browser tabs pre-opened: demo workflow, proof point slide, pricing one-pager
- Notifications silenced on demo machine

### What to Customize Before the Demo

This is the most important pre-demo step. Spend 20 minutes doing this. It changes everything.

**For all demos:**
- Replace "Example Business" with their actual business name throughout the workflow
- Replace generic contact info with their suburb/city if shown
- Change any industry-specific language to match their sector

**For Track A demos:**
- Set the "incoming message" trigger to the channel they actually use (WhatsApp vs. web form vs. email)
- Customize the automated response to sound like it fits their brand tone (formal vs. casual)
- If they mentioned a specific booking system, reference it in the handoff step

**For Track B demos:**
- Name the workflow after a real process they described (e.g., "Weekly Roster Distribution" or "Client Invoice Follow-Up Sequence")
- If they use Xero, show Xero in the tool chain. If they use QuickBooks, show QuickBooks.
- Match the number of steps in the demo to the number of steps they said they currently do manually

**For Track C demos:**
- Brief the AI agent script to use their actual service offerings if possible
- Reference their actual call types (e.g., "appointment booking" vs. "quote requests")

## Demo Narrative A: After-Hours Lead Loss (Engagement Engine)

**Target:** Wellness clinics, restaurants, beauty salons, service businesses, trades, real estate agents, gyms, physiotherapy practices, legal offices.

**Core Problem:** Leads come in after 5pm or on weekends. Nobody responds until Monday morning. By then, the prospect has booked with whoever replied first. The business owner does not know how much revenue they are losing because they never see the missed opportunities.

**Engine:** Engagement Engine — $150/month + $899 setup

---

### Opening Frame

Do not open with features. Open with their world.

Say this (adapt to their business type):

> "Before I show you anything, I want to replay something that probably happened to your business last Friday night. Someone found your clinic online, filled out your contact form at 11pm, and waited. They woke up Saturday morning with no reply. By the time you saw it Monday, they'd already booked somewhere else. You never knew that lead existed. Let me show you what that same sequence looks like when Autoflow is running."

Pause. Let that land.

Then: "I'm going to share my screen and walk you through this in real time."

---

### Live Demo Flow

**Step 1 — Trigger the incoming message**

Show a contact form submission or WhatsApp message coming in with a timestamp of 11:14pm. Use their business name on the form. If you know their actual form fields (name, service type, preferred time), replicate them.

Say: "This is 11:14pm on a Friday. Your team has been off for six hours. Here's what the Engagement Engine sees."

**Step 2 — Show the AI parsing the message**

Show the workflow reading the message and extracting the intent: new lead, service type, preferred contact method.

Say: "It reads the message, understands the intent, and in under three seconds it does three things automatically."

**Step 3 — The three automatic actions**

Show these firing in sequence:

1. Instant reply sent to the lead (WhatsApp, SMS, or email depending on their channel) confirming receipt and offering a booking link or next step
2. The lead is logged in their CRM or a Google Sheet (match to what they actually use)
3. You (the business owner) receive a summary notification so you see it first thing in the morning

Say: "The lead gets an instant reply. They feel acknowledged. They do not go looking for your competitor. You wake up Saturday morning with a qualified lead already in your pipeline and a booking already tentatively scheduled."

**Step 4 — Show the morning follow-up**

Show the automated 8am follow-up message firing if the lead has not yet confirmed the booking.

Say: "If they did not confirm overnight, the system follows up at 8am. You have not lifted a finger. This runs whether you are on holiday, at a weekend game with your kids, or just not at your desk."

---

### The Aha Moment

This is the moment you want to create. Ask them to watch the timestamp on the response.

> "Watch this. Message comes in at 11:14pm. Response goes out at 11:14pm. Three seconds. That lead just had a better experience with your business at 11pm than most businesses deliver at 2pm on a Tuesday."

Then: "How many leads do you think you've lost in the last six months because nobody replied fast enough?"

Do not answer the question for them. Let them calculate it silently.

### Proof Point Integration

Within the first month, their no-show rate dropped 40% because the automated confirmation and reminder sequence kept leads warm from the moment they enquired. They stopped losing leads they never knew they had."

## POC / Pilot Design

### When to Offer a Pilot

Offer a pilot when you see these specific signals:

- High genuine interest but decision-making hesitation ("I like it, but I need to be sure")
- Multiple decision-makers involved who were not on the demo call
- Business is technically risk-averse or has been burned by a previous vendor
- They want to see it on their actual tools and data before committing to a full retainer
- The deal is large enough (multiple engines or Execution Engine) that a pilot makes commercial sense

Do not offer a pilot to every prospect. It is a tool for specific situations, not a default fallback.

### What a 30-Day Pilot Looks Like

**Engagement Engine Pilot:**

Scope: One automation workflow targeting a single contact channel (e.g., web form only, or WhatsApp only).
Goal: Capture and respond to 100% of after-hours enquiries for 30 days.
Success metric: Number of leads captured after hours vs. previous 30 days. Conversion rate on those leads.
Reporting: Weekly summary of all contacts handled, response times, leads logged.

**Operations Engine Pilot:**

Scope: One recurring workflow (e.g., weekly invoice follow-up, or monthly roster distribution).
Goal: Run the workflow automatically without human input for four consecutive cycles.
Success metric: Hours saved vs. previous manual process. Accuracy of outputs.
Reporting: Log of every run with inputs, outputs, and any exceptions flagged.

**Voice Engine Pilot:**

Scope: After-hours inbound calls only (not replacing daytime reception).
Goal: Handle all after-hours calls for 30 days with zero missed calls.
Success metric: Number of calls handled, bookings converted, escalations required.
Reporting: Call log with duration, outcome, and escalation flag for each call.

### How to Scope the Pilot So It Proves ROI Within 30 Days

A poorly scoped pilot proves nothing. A well-scoped pilot makes the full retainer a formality.

Rules for scoping:

1. Choose the workflow with the highest frequency. A weekly process gives you 4 data points in 30 days. A monthly process gives you 1. More runs equals more proof.
2. Define the success metric in advance and get them to agree before the pilot starts. "We'll consider this a success if [specific outcome]." Do not leave this open.
3. Focus on the pain they rated highest during the Friction Audit, not the easiest workflow to automate.
4. Make sure the baseline is measurable. If they cannot tell you how many after-hours leads they currently miss, establish a tracking method first.

### Pilot Pricing and Positioning

The pilot is not free. Free trials train clients to expect free work. They also create poor-quality engagement because there is no financial commitment on either side.

**Standard pilot pricing:**

| Engine | Pilot Price (30 days) | Includes |
|---|---|---|
| Engagement Engine | $499 | Build, testing, 30-day operation, weekly reporting, review call |
| Operations Engine | $699 | Build, testing, 30 days of operation, weekly reporting, review call |
| Voice Engine | $799 | Build, testing, 30 days of operation, call logs, review call |

**How to position the pilot price:**

> "The pilot is a paid engagement at a reduced rate. It covers the build and the first 30 days of operation. It is not a free trial because we are doing real work: building your actual workflow, connected to your actual tools, running on your real data. What you pay for the pilot is credited against the setup fee if you convert to a full retainer within 14 days of the pilot ending. So if it works, you effectively get a discounted setup."

### How to Convert a Pilot to a Full Retainer

At day 21 of the pilot, send a pilot review email with:

- What has run over the past 21 days
- The metrics vs. the agreed success metric
- A "what the next 12 months looks like" projection based on the data

Book a 20-minute review call for day 28. In that call:

1. Walk through the results together
2. Ask: "Based on what you've seen, does this solve the problem?"
3. If yes: "The retainer is [price]/month. I'll send the agreement today and we keep running without interruption."
4. If hesitation: "What would you need to see in the next 30 days to feel confident? Let's define that now and I'll extend the pilot to cover it."

Do not wait until the pilot ends to start the conversion conversation. Start it at day 21.

### What "Custom-Built" Means in Practice

When Autoflow says "custom-built," here is exactly what that means:

It is not a template dropped into your account. Every workflow is architected from scratch based on:

1. Your specific process (mapped during discovery)
2. Your specific tools (connected via API or webhook)
3. Your specific business rules (the logic you currently apply manually)
4. Your specific outputs (what needs to be created, sent, or logged)
5. Your escalation rules (what goes to a human and when)

Two accounting firms can have identical tool stacks and completely different workflows because their processes, staff structures, and client types differ. We build for the real process, not a hypothetical one.

Custom-built also means the logic layer is designed for your edge cases. What happens when a client has two overdue invoices? What happens when a lead submits the form twice? What happens when the booking system returns an error? These are the scenarios templates ignore and custom architecture handles.

## Technical Close Script

### How to End the Demo with a Clear Next Step

Never end a demo with "let me know what you think." That is an invitation to go cold. End with a specific, time-bound next action that you control.

The demo should end in one of two states:

**State 1: Ready to move forward**

Signals: They are asking about timelines, setup, onboarding, pricing details, or saying "this is exactly what we need."

**State 2: Needs more information or a second stakeholder**

Signals: They say "I need to show my partner," "let me think about it," "can you send me more info," or they go quiet.

---

### Word-for-Word Transition to Proposal Stage

**If State 1 (ready to move forward):**

> "Great. Based on everything we've covered, I'm going to put together a one-page proposal for [their business name] specifically. It will show the exact workflow architecture for the [engine name], the tools we'll connect, the timeline, and the investment. I can have that in your inbox by [specific time tomorrow]. From there, if it looks right to you, the next step is a signed agreement and a 50% deposit to kick off the build. Realistically, you'd be live in [X to Y] business days. Does [specific date] as a go-live target sound workable for you?"

Confirm the email address. Set a specific time you will send the proposal ("I'll send it by 2pm tomorrow"). Then send it.

**If State 2 (needs more or second stakeholder):**

> "That makes sense. Here's what I'd suggest: let me send you a one-pager by tomorrow that you can share with [partner/team]. It lays out exactly what we're proposing, the investment, and the timeline. Then let's book a 20-minute call with [all decision-makers] for later this week. That way you're not trying to relay a technical conversation. I'll do that directly. Does Thursday work for a short call?"

Get a specific date and time for the follow-up call. Do not leave it as "reach out whenever."

---

### How to Handle "We Need to See More"

If they say this after the demo, it means one of three things:

1. The demo was not specific enough to their situation (most common)
2. There is a technical concern they have not verbalized
3. They are genuinely interested but cautious and need the pilot framing

**Script:**

> "Completely fair. What specifically would be most useful to see? Is it more about how it integrates with [their tools], or is it more about seeing it run on your actual data?"

If it is about their tools or data: transition to pilot.

If it is about something else they name: address it directly, then re-close.

> "That's a good point. Here's how we handle that: [specific answer]. Does that resolve that concern, or is there another piece you want to get clear on before we move forward?"

---

### How to Handle "We're Ready to Move Forward"

Resist the urge to keep talking. When someone says they're ready, the next words out of your mouth should be about the agreement, not more information about the product.

> "Excellent. I'll send the proposal and agreement together this afternoon. Once you've reviewed it and it looks right, you sign and send back the deposit. I'll book the kickoff call for [specific date], and we'll be in build mode by end of week. You good to move that fast?"

Speed signals confidence. If you drag the close out, doubt creeps in.

---

### Post-Demo Follow-Up Sequence

If a prospect does not respond to the proposal within 48 hours, follow up with this sequence:

**Day 2 (48 hours after proposal sent):**

> "Hi [name], just checking the proposal came through clearly. Happy to jump on a quick call if any questions came up. Does [specific time] work today or tomorrow?"

**Day 5:**

> "Hi [name], I wanted to follow up before I close out this proposal. We have a build slot opening up [date] and I'd rather hold it for you than give it to the next enquiry. Let me know where your head is at."

**Day 10:**

> "Hi [name], last follow-up from me on this. The slot I mentioned is now allocated. If the timing was the issue, I'd rather revisit in [X weeks] when it works better for you. Worth a quick reply either way so I know where things stand."

After day 10, move the lead to a 30-day nurture sequence and do not follow up manually again until that sequence completes.
