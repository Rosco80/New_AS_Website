# Deal Strategy & MEDDPICC Qualification System — Autoflow Solutions

---

## Overview

### Why MEDDPICC Matters for an SME-Focused Sales Motion

Autoflow Solutions sells to business owners running companies with 5 to 100 employees. These are not enterprise deals with procurement committees, legal reviews, and 90-day evaluation cycles. Most deals close in 14 to 35 days. The owner is the buyer, the user, and often the implementer champion all in one.

So why use MEDDPICC? Because the biggest threat to your pipeline is not losing to a competitor. It is wasting 12 hours of discovery, proposal writing, and follow-up on a prospect who was never going to buy. SME owners are busy. They will take your calls, enjoy your demo, and nod along to your pitch, then disappear. Not because they were dishonest. Because they have 11 other problems to solve this week.

MEDDPICC is a filter, not a sales trick. It tells you in the first two conversations whether a deal is real or a mirage. It prevents you from filling your pipeline with happy listeners who have no urgency, no budget, and no plan to change anything. At Autoflow's deal volume, one bad deal in the pipeline costs you two weeks of opportunity cost.

The adapted approach for Autoflow strips out enterprise complexity and keeps only what applies to a single-decision-maker SME sale. Champion, for example, matters only when the owner has an ops manager who is doing the actual workflow pain. Decision Process is short but it still exists: some owners say "let me run it by my business partner" at proposal stage, which is a deal killer if you did not know about it on day one.

### Deal Scoring Philosophy

Every deal in your pipeline gets a MEDDPICC score from 0 to 100 before a proposal is written. This is not bureaucracy. It is a hard gate.

**If a deal scores below 50, do not send a proposal.**

A proposal is not a discovery tool. It is a commitment of your time and an implicit promise to the prospect that they are ready to buy. Sending a proposal to a 38-point deal trains the prospect to expect the output without providing the input (budget confirmation, timeline, specific pain). It also signals that your qualification standards are low, which erodes perceived value.

Below 50 means: go back to discovery, or park the deal and move on.

## MEDDPICC Scorecard for Autoflow Deals

### Scoring Key (per element)
- **2 points:** Strong, confirmed signal
- **1 point:** Partial signal, needs follow-up
- **0 points:** No signal, missing or vague

---

### M — Metrics: Can They Quantify the Problem?

**What this means at Autoflow:**
Metrics are the numbers that make the pain real. For an SME owner, this means they can tell you how many hours per week their team spends on the task you are automating, what that time costs them in salary or opportunity, and what a measurable improvement looks like. If they cannot put a number on the problem, the ROI argument in your proposal lands in a vacuum.

**Strong signal (score 2):**
The prospect says something like: "My operations manager spends 15 hours a week doing follow-up emails. At $25 an hour, that is $375 a week I am burning on repetitive work." Or: "We miss about 8 inbound leads a week because no one picks up after 5pm. Our average client is worth $3,000." They have already done the math or they do it with you on the call.

**Weak signal (score 0 to 1):**
The prospect says "it takes a lot of time" or "it feels manual" but cannot give a number. They agree the problem exists but frame it in feelings rather than figures. Score 1 if they can estimate loosely ("maybe 10 hours?") but have not confirmed it. Score 0 if the answer is "I am not sure, it is just annoying."

**Qualifying question:**
"If you think about the specific task you want to automate, roughly how many hours per week does your team spend on it right now? And what does that cost you, either in wages or in leads you are not catching?"

---

### E — Economic Buyer: Is the Person You Are Talking to the One Who Signs?

**What this means at Autoflow:**
In an SME, the economic buyer is almost always the owner or founder. But "almost always" is not "always." You may be talking to an operations manager, a practice manager, or a general manager who has influence but not signing authority. Confirming this early prevents a proposal from dying the silent death of "I need to check with my business partner."

**Strong signal (score 2):**
The person you are speaking with explicitly confirms they own the budget and can commit without approval. "Yes, this is my decision. I own the P&L." Or: you have already identified through LinkedIn, the website, or the intake form that they are the founder.

**Weak signal (score 0 to 1):**
They say "I would need to loop in my partner" or "I would need to get sign-off." Or you are speaking to someone with a title like "Office Manager" or "Operations Lead" who has not confirmed decision authority. Score 1 if they are clearly influential and you have a path to the owner. Score 0 if you have never spoken to or confirmed the actual buyer.

**Qualifying question:**
"When a decision like this comes up, who is typically involved in the final call? Is that you, or do you have a business partner or co-founder who would need to weigh in?"

---

### D — Decision Criteria: What Does a "Yes" Look Like for Them?

**What this means at Autoflow:**
Decision criteria are the specific conditions the prospect is using to evaluate whether Autoflow is the right choice. This is different from pain. Pain is why they want to change. Decision criteria are how they will decide what to change to. For SME owners, criteria are often a mix of: price threshold, simplicity of setup, speed to results, and trust in the vendor.

**Strong signal (score 2):**
The prospect has articulated specific requirements: "I need this running within two weeks, I cannot spend more than $300 a month, and I need to know you can handle our CRM which is HubSpot." Or they have told you what made them say no to a previous tool or vendor. Clear, specific, and tied to their situation.

**Weak signal (score 0 to 1):**
They say "I just want something that works" or "price is important but so is quality." Vague criteria that do not differentiate. Score 1 if they have mentioned one or two factors loosely. Score 0 if you have not asked and they have not volunteered anything specific.

**Qualifying question:**
"When you picture this working perfectly in six months, what does that look like? And what would make you say no to something, even if it solved the problem?"

---

### D — Decision Process: How Do They Make Buying Decisions?

**What this means at Autoflow:**
Even simple SME deals have a process. The owner might want to see a demo before a proposal. They might want one week to test a trial. They might need to discuss with their accountant. Understanding the steps between "interested" and "signed" prevents surprises at proposal stage.

**Strong signal (score 2):**
The prospect has mapped out their own process: "I like to see the demo, then I would want a written proposal I can review over the weekend, then I am ready to commit." Or they have bought a SaaS tool before and they describe a predictable pattern. You know exactly what needs to happen and in what order.

**Weak signal (score 0 to 1):**
They say "I am just exploring right now" without a timeline or next step. Or the process is unclear and they have not engaged with scoping the path to a decision. Score 1 if there is a loose indication of timing but no defined steps. Score 0 if there is no timeline and no process visible.

**Qualifying question:**
"Assuming the demo ticks all your boxes and the numbers make sense, what would the next step look like for you? How quickly could you move if you felt confident?"

---

### I — Identify Pain: Is the Pain Specific, Quantified, and Actively Felt?

**What this means at Autoflow:**
This is the most important element. Pain at Autoflow is not abstract. It is a specific workflow that is breaking, costing money, or preventing growth. The prospect needs to name the pain, own it, and feel it now, not in some future state. Latent pain ("we should probably automate this someday") is not the same as active pain ("this is costing me money every week and I am done waiting").

**Strong signal (score 2):**
The prospect names a specific process (e.g., "our lead follow-up is completely manual"), ties it to a cost or consequence ("we lost three deals last month because nobody followed up fast enough"), and has expressed urgency ("I need to fix this before our busy season in April"). The pain is named, measured, and urgent.

**Weak signal (score 0 to 1):**
They agree there is a problem but it feels more like background noise than a burning fire. "We know we could be more efficient" is not pain. Score 1 if they have identified the problem but not the cost or urgency. Score 0 if the pain is vague or entirely future-oriented.

**Qualifying question:**
"What is the most painful part of this right now? What happens to your business if this problem is still there six months from now?"

---

### C — Champion: Is There Someone Internally Who Wants This to Win?

**What this means at Autoflow:**
In enterprise sales, champions are the internal advocates who sell up the chain. In SME deals, the champion is usually the owner themselves, which simplifies things. But in businesses with 20 or more employees, there is often an ops manager, practice manager, or team lead who lives inside the broken process every day. That person's buy-in matters. If the owner is interested but the ops manager is skeptical, the deal dies in implementation, not in signing.

**Strong signal (score 2):**
The owner is enthusiastic and personally invested. Or you have spoken with the ops manager and they are actively pushing for the solution. Someone is running toward this deal, not just considering it.

**Weak signal (score 0 to 1):**
The owner seems mildly interested but not driven. You have heard phrases like "it might be good to look at" or "let me think about it." No one is internally advocating for moving forward. Score 1 if the owner is engaged but not urgent. Score 0 if you are pulling harder than they are.

**Qualifying question:**
"Who on your team is most frustrated by this problem right now? Would it be useful to bring them into a conversation so they can ask questions about the implementation?"

---

### C — Competition: What Are They Comparing You To?

**What this means at Autoflow:**
Almost every SME prospect is comparing Autoflow to one of three things: building it themselves with Zapier or Make, hiring an admin or VA, or doing nothing and staying with the status quo. Understanding which competition you are up against determines which win strategy to deploy. Not asking this question means you show up to the proposal with the wrong argument.

**Strong signal (score 2):**
The prospect has named a specific alternative they are considering: "We looked at Zapier but it seemed complicated" or "We interviewed a VA last month." You know exactly what you are competing against and you have already started addressing it.

**Weak signal (score 0 to 1):**
They have not mentioned alternatives and have not been asked. Or they said "we have not really looked at anything else" which may mean no urgency. Score 1 if there is a named alternative but you have not addressed it yet. Score 0 if competition is completely unknown.

**Qualifying question:**
"Before you found Autoflow, what other options were you looking at? Have you tried anything like Zapier or Make, or were you thinking about hiring someone to handle this?"

## Deal Scoring Model (0 to 100)

### Score Interpretation

| Score Range | Status | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 70 to 100 | Priority Deal | Build close plan now. Send proposal this week. Full pursuit. |
| 50 to 69 | Develop More | One more discovery call focused on missing elements. Do not send proposal yet. |
| Below 50 | Disqualify or Park | Do not send proposal. Either re-engage with targeted questions or move to nurture. |

### When to Send a Proposal vs. Schedule Another Discovery Call

**Send a proposal when:**
- MEDDPICC score is 70 or above
- Pain is specific and quantified (score of 2 on Identify Pain)
- Economic Buyer is confirmed (score of 2 on Economic Buyer)
- You know what you are competing against
- A verbal commitment exists: "If the proposal looks right, I am ready to move forward"

**Schedule another discovery call when:**
- Score is 50 to 69
- Pain is identified but not quantified
- Decision process is unclear or a mystery stakeholder has appeared
- The competition is unknown

**Park or disqualify when:**
- Score is below 50
- The prospect cannot name a specific problem
- No timeline exists and no urgency has been expressed
- You are the only one working to keep this deal alive

### The 3-Point Pain Confirmation Before Any Proposal

Before writing a single line of proposal, confirm all three:

1. "Have we agreed on the specific problem and what it costs you right now?"
2. "Do you have budget allocated for this kind of solution, or do we need to work through that?"
3. "If the proposal looks right, when would you want to start?"

If you cannot get clear answers to all three, the deal is not ready for a proposal.

## Five Deal Stages with Exit Criteria

### Stage 1: Signal Identified

**Definition:** An outbound trigger has fired (job posting, new location, negative review, hiring ad) or an inbound lead has arrived (website form, referral, content download).

**What must be true to exit Stage 1:**
- Contact has been identified and verified as a decision-maker or has direct access to one
- A first conversation or response has occurred
- The lead source has been noted (outbound trigger vs. inbound vs. referral)
- A Friction Audit call has been scheduled

**What moves it forward:**
- A personalized first outreach that names the specific pain tied to the trigger signal
- A direct question that creates a reason to respond ("I noticed you have three five-star reviews about response time, but your Google listing shows you are closed on Sundays. Is after-hours lead capture a gap for you?")
- A calendar link in the first or second message

**What causes it to stall:**
- Generic outreach that could apply to any business
- No clear hook or reason the prospect should respond
- Too much information in the first message (no call to action)
- Waiting more than 24 hours to follow up after an inbound inquiry

**Stall recovery:**
Send a pattern-interrupt follow-up focused on cost of inaction. "Just checking in, not because I want a call for its own sake, but because we calculated the average cost of manual lead follow-up for a business your size is about $1,400 a month. If that number is off for you, tell me and I will stop. If it sounds close, it is worth 20 minutes."

---

### Stage 2: Friction Audit Completed

**Definition:** A discovery call has been completed. The prospect has described their current workflow, the specific pain, and their business context. MEDDPICC scoring has been started.

**What must be true to exit Stage 2:**
- At least 4 of 7 MEDDPICC elements have been scored at 1 or above
- A specific pain has been named and partially quantified
- The economic buyer has been confirmed or identified
- A MEDDPICC score of at least 50 has been reached
- A demo or solution presentation has been scheduled

**What moves it forward:**
- Asking the six core qualifying questions from the MEDDPICC scorecard
- Turning the discovery call into a working session: "Let me help you calculate what this is actually costing you right now"
- Summarizing the call back to the prospect in writing ("Based on our call, here is what I heard...") and asking for a correction or confirmation

**What causes it to stall:**
- Prospect agrees with everything but commits to nothing
- Discovery call was mostly you talking, not the prospect
- No follow-up summary was sent within 24 hours of the call
- Score is below 50 but a demo was still scheduled anyway

**Stall recovery:**
Send a written friction summary that forces a reaction. Name the specific pain, the cost you calculated together, and ask one direct question: "Did I get that right, or is the problem bigger or smaller than what I described?" This either re-engages them or confirms the deal was never real.

---

### Stage 3: Demo and Solution Presented

**Definition:** A live demo or solution walkthrough has been delivered, tailored to the specific pain uncovered in Stage 2. The prospect has seen what Autoflow would build for them.

**What must be true to exit Stage 3:**
- The demo was personalized to their industry and specific workflow
- Objections were surfaced and handled (not dodged)
- The competitive situation was addressed directly
- A verbal interest level has been established ("On a scale of 1 to 10, how interested are you in moving forward?")
- MEDDPICC score has been updated and is at 70 or above
- A proposal timeline has been agreed: "I will send the proposal by Thursday, and I would like to schedule a 20-minute review call for Friday"

**What moves it forward:**
- Doing a live ROI calculation during the demo using their own numbers
- Asking the prospect to verbally describe the problem back to you using the Autoflow framing
- Naming the competition directly: "You mentioned you were looking at Zapier. Can I show you specifically why what we build is different?"
- Proposing a concrete next step with a date attached

**What causes it to stall:**
- Demo was generic rather than customized to their workflow
- No next step was agreed before the call ended
- The ROI case was not made in their language and their numbers
- An objection came up and was not fully resolved before the call ended

**Stall recovery:**
Send a post-demo recap with a specific ROI summary. "Based on what you told me: 12 hours per week on follow-up, 3 missed leads a week at $2,000 average value. The Engagement Engine at $150/month pays for itself in the first week. I am holding a proposal slot for you through Friday. Do you want me to proceed?"

---

### Stage 4: Proposal Sent

**Definition:** A written proposal has been delivered. Pricing, scope, and terms are on the table.

**What must be true to exit Stage 4:**
- The proposal review call has happened (never send a proposal without scheduling the review call in advance)
- All objections from the review call have been addressed
- A clear decision timeline has been confirmed
- The prospect has said yes, asked for a revision, or given a definitive no

**What moves it forward:**
- The proposal review call (not "did you get a chance to read it?")
- A specific decision date on the calendar
- A risk-removal element: the ROI guarantee ("If it does not save 10 hours per week or $2,000 per month, we do not build it")
- Making the next step simple and low-friction: "All I need is a reply to this email and a signed one-page agreement. Setup starts the following Monday."

**What causes it to stall:**
- Proposal was sent without a review call booked
- The proposal had too much generic information and not enough specificity to their situation
- No urgency mechanism exists (no limited availability, no deadline)
- The prospect ghosted after reading it

**Stall recovery:**
One direct follow-up, not a nudge. "I sent the proposal on [date]. I want to respect your time, so I am going to be direct: are you still interested in moving forward, or has something changed? Either answer is fine, I just need to know so I can manage my schedule."

---

### Stage 5: Closed (Won or Lost)

**Won — what must be true:**
- Agreement signed
- Setup fee paid or invoiced
- Kickoff call scheduled
- CRM updated with deal source, engine purchased, and close reason

**Lost — what must be documented:**
- Specific loss reason (competitor, price, timing, no decision, lost to inertia)
- MEDDPICC score at time of loss (this teaches you where the deal was weak)
- Whether the deal is a future opportunity (nurture vs. permanent loss)
- A brief post-mortem note for pipeline review

**Closed-Lost follow-up:**
Send one message 90 days after a loss. "I know the timing was not right earlier this year. A lot of the businesses we work with in [their industry] are now seeing [specific result from an active client]. If things have changed on your end, I am happy to revisit. If not, no follow-up needed."

## Competitive Win Strategies

---

### Scenario 1: They Are Comparing to Zapier, Make, or DIY Tools

**Their objection:** "We could probably just build this ourselves in Zapier. It does not seem that complicated."

**Why this happens:** The prospect has heard of no-code automation tools and assumes the skill and time required are low. They are anchoring on the build cost, not the total cost of ownership.

**Challenger Teaching Sequence:**

Step 1: Acknowledge the tool. Do not dismiss Zapier. It is a legitimate tool. "Zapier is great for simple one-step automations. If you want to auto-forward an email, it works perfectly."

Step 2: Introduce the hidden complexity. "The challenge is that what you described, a multi-step intake, qualification, CRM update, and follow-up sequence with conditional logic, is not a one-step automation. The average setup for something like that in Zapier is 15 to 20 hours for someone who already knows the platform. Have you used Zapier before?"

Step 3: Introduce the maintenance burden. "Here is the part most people do not account for: Zapier automations break. When a connected app updates its API, when a field name changes in your CRM, when you add a new team member and their access level is different, the automation stops working. You will not always know it stopped. Missed leads and dropped follow-ups happen silently. Our clients who came from DIY tools estimate they spent 4 to 6 hours a month on maintenance before they switched."

Step 4: Do the total cost of ownership math live.

| Cost Component | DIY Zapier | Autoflow |
|---|---|---|
| Initial build time (at owner's hourly rate, $75/hr) | $1,125 to $1,500 | $0 (we build it) |
| Monthly maintenance (4 hrs at $75/hr) | $300/month | $0 (we maintain it) |
| Monthly tool cost (Zapier Professional) | $49/month | Included |
| Monthly retainer | $0 | $150/month |
| **Year 1 total cost** | **$4,713 to $5,088** | **$2,699** |

Setup fee: $899. Monthly: $150. Year 1 total: $899 + (12 x $150) = $2,699.

**Win move:** Present this table during the demo. Do not email it cold. Build it with them using their numbers. When they see their own hourly rate in the calculation, the comparison becomes concrete.

**Script for the close:**
"I am not saying Zapier cannot do this. It can. The question is whether you want to spend the next few months becoming a Zapier developer, or whether you want the automation running next week and someone else maintaining it for the cost of a monthly gym membership. Which of those is a better use of your time?"

### Scenario 2: They Are Comparing to Hiring an Admin or VA

**Their objection:** "We were actually thinking about hiring a part-time admin to handle some of this. It might be more personal."

**Why this happens:** The prospect is comfortable with the idea of a human doing the work. They associate automation with impersonal experiences. They also underestimate the true cost of employment.

**The Salary Math:**

| Cost Component | Part-Time Admin (20 hrs/week) | Autoflow Operations Engine |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly salary | $1,600 to $2,400 | $0 |
| Employer payroll taxes (10-15%) | $160 to $360 | $0 |
| Recruitment / onboarding | $500 to $2,000 (one-time) | $1,699 (one-time setup) |
| Training time (owner's hours) | 20 to 40 hrs | 2 hrs kickoff call |
| Sick days, turnover risk | Yes | No |
| 24/7 availability | No | Yes |
| Monthly software cost | $0 | $170/month |
| **Year 1 total cost** | **$22,320 to $35,520** | **$3,739** |

Year 1 Autoflow: $1,699 + (12 x $170) = $3,739.

**The Leverage Argument:**

Script: "A great admin is genuinely valuable for judgment-heavy work: client relationships, complex decisions, creative problem-solving. But the work you are describing, the intake forms, the follow-up emails, the appointment reminders, the CRM updates, is repetitive and rule-based. It does not need judgment. It needs consistency. An AI agent does that exact work for $170 a month, never calls in sick, works at 2am if a lead comes in at 2am, and never quits three weeks before your busy season. The admin you hire becomes more valuable when they do not have to do any of that."

**Win move:** Ask the prospect to name the specific tasks they would assign to the admin. Then show them which of those tasks the Operations Engine handles. Make the scope overlap visual. The remaining high-judgment tasks become the case for keeping (or hiring) a human who is no longer doing repetitive work.

**Script for the objection about "personal touch":**
"I hear you on the personal connection piece. Here is how our clients handle it: Autoflow handles the first 80% of the touchpoints, the instant acknowledgment, the scheduling, the reminder sequence. By the time the prospect gets to a human conversation, they feel taken care of. Your admin, or you, shows up to a warm conversation instead of a cold intake. The experience is actually more personal because you are not rushing through admin work to get to the relationship part."

### Scenario 3: They Are Comparing to Doing Nothing (Inertia)

**Their objection:** "We are honestly managing fine. It is not perfect but it works."

**Why this happens:** The cost of the problem is invisible because it is distributed across time. The owner has normalized the inefficiency. There is no acute crisis, so there is no urgency.

**The Inaction Tax Calculation:**

This is the most important reframe in Autoflow's competitive playbook. You are not competing with a product. You are competing with the mental inertia of "good enough."

Script: "I want to show you something. You said you are managing fine, and I believe you. Managing fine means the wheels are on. But let me ask: how many hours a week does your team spend on [the specific task]?"

Wait for a number. Let them say it out loud.

"So 12 hours a week. At [their team's hourly rate], that is [calculated cost] per week. Over a year, that is [annual cost]. I am not telling you that to alarm you. I am telling you because that is what you are already spending. The question is not whether you can afford Autoflow. The question is whether you want to keep paying [annual cost] to do this manually."

**The Inaction Tax Table (example with 12 hrs/week at $25/hr):**

| Time Frame | Cost of Inaction |
|---|---|
| Per week | $300 |
| Per month | $1,300 |
| Per year | $15,600 |
| Autoflow year 1 cost | $2,699 |
| **Net saving in year 1** | **$12,901** |

**The ROI Guarantee as a Risk-Removal Close:**

"Here is our commitment to you. If after our friction audit we cannot demonstrate with your own numbers that Autoflow will save at least 10 hours per week or $2,000 per month, we will not build it. We do not want to take your money for something that does not pay for itself. We have turned down clients because the math did not work. Does that change how you are thinking about the risk?"

**What this does:** It removes the fear of a bad investment by making the decision criteria explicit and the risk theirs (Autoflow's) rather than the prospect's.

**Script for the "we tried automation before" variant:**
"What did you use? [Listen.] That makes sense. Most off-the-shelf tools like [name] are template-based. They are built to work for 80% of businesses, which means they fit your workflow 80% of the way, and that last 20% is where they break. What we build is custom to your specific intake flow, your CRM, your follow-up logic. The reason it holds up is that it was designed for your business, not adapted from someone else's."

## Multi-Threading Map

### Primary Contact: Business Owner / CEO / Founder

This is almost always your main contact and your economic buyer in one. For companies under 15 people, the owner is also the ops manager, the sales lead, and the person who will be using the reporting dashboard. Keep the relationship here.

**Engagement approach:**
- Lead every call and email with business outcomes, not technical features
- Use the owner's language: revenue, time, headcount, stress
- Keep communications short: owners do not read long emails
- Always include a single clear next step with a date

### Secondary Contact: Ops Manager or Practice Manager

In businesses with 20 to 100 employees, there is often a second decision influencer. This person lives inside the broken process every day. They know exactly how many hours it takes. They may be the one who pitched the owner on exploring Autoflow. Or they may be the one who will sabotage implementation if they feel their role is being threatened.

**Engagement approach:**
- Bring the ops manager into the discovery call, not the demo
- Frame their involvement as expertise: "I would love to get 20 minutes with [name] to understand the workflow from the inside before I build the demo. They will have details you might not think to mention."
- Never position Autoflow as a replacement for the ops manager. Position it as a tool that removes the work they hate so they can focus on the work they are good at.
- After the deal closes, the ops manager is your implementation champion. Keep them informed.

### How to Engage Both Without Going Around the Primary

The rule is transparency. Tell the owner you want to speak with the ops manager. Do not make contact independently. "I want to make sure the solution I build fits the actual workflow, not just the description of it. Would it be okay if I had a 20-minute technical discovery call with [name] before the demo? You are welcome to join or I can send you the notes."

This respects the owner's authority while unlocking the ops manager's knowledge.

### When Multi-Threading Matters in SME Deals

Multi-threading becomes critical in two situations:

1. **At the friction audit stage** (20 to 100 employee companies): You need the ops manager's workflow knowledge to build an accurate demo. If your demo is based only on the owner's description, it will miss edge cases.

2. **At the close stage**: If the owner has expressed interest but seems stuck, the ops manager's enthusiasm can be the push that closes it. A message like "I spoke with [ops manager] and they are excited to get started" gives the owner permission to say yes.

In very small businesses (5 to 15 employees), multi-threading is usually unnecessary. The owner is the full picture.

## Challenger Teaching Sequences

Challenger selling works in Autoflow deals because most SME owners have a mental model of automation that is incomplete. They learned about automation from product-led tools designed for technical users. Your job is to teach them something they did not know, which positions you as the expert rather than the vendor.

### Teaching Sequence 1: "AI Automation Is Complicated to Manage"

**The misconception:** "We are interested but we worry that AI automation will require a lot of technical upkeep. We do not have an IT person."

**The reframe:** Done-for-you vs. self-managed.

Step 1 — Validate: "That concern makes complete sense if you are thinking about tools like Zapier or Make, or even custom GPT prompts. Those require someone to maintain them."

Step 2 — Teach: "The model Autoflow uses is different. We are not selling you software. We are selling you an outcome. The agents we build are monitored by us. When something breaks or a connected tool updates, we fix it. You do not see the technical layer at all. Your interaction is a monthly report showing how many leads were captured, how many follow-ups were sent, and how much time was saved."

Step 3 — Proof: "Our clients typically spend less than 30 minutes a month interacting with the system: reviewing the report, flagging any edge cases. That is it."

Step 4 — Bridge: "Does that change the concern, or is there a specific part of the management that you were worried about?"

---

### Teaching Sequence 2: "We Tried Automation Before and It Broke"

**The misconception:** "We set up some Zapier automations two years ago and they kept failing. We eventually just gave up."

**The reframe:** Custom-built versus template-adapted tools.

Step 1 — Validate: "That is one of the most common things we hear, and it is completely fair. A lot of businesses have a bad first experience with automation for exactly that reason."

Step 2 — Teach the difference: "The reason template tools break is that they are designed for the average workflow. They connect apps A to B using a standard path. But your workflow is not average. You have specific conditions, edge cases, CRM fields that do not match the default, follow-up logic that changes based on the lead source. When the template hits a condition it was not built for, it fails silently."

Step 3 — Contrast: "What we build is specific to your exact workflow. We map every edge case in the friction audit before we write a line of code. The output is an agent that knows your business logic, not a generic template."

Step 4 — The trust bridge: "I can also show you what a failed automation looks like in our system versus a generic tool. The difference in how they handle exceptions is the difference between 99% uptime and 60% uptime. Would that be useful?"

---

### Teaching Sequence 3: "We Are Not a Tech Company"

**The misconception:** "This sounds impressive but AI automation feels like it is for tech companies or big enterprises. We are just a [plumber / dental practice / law firm / trades business]."

**The reframe:** Autoflow specifically serves non-tech SMEs.

Step 1 — Validate: "You are right that most AI tools are designed for tech companies. The interfaces are technical, the setup requires developers, and the pricing assumes a software budget."

Step 2 — Flip the frame: "Autoflow was specifically built for exactly the kind of business you run. The businesses that benefit most from this are not the ones with a dev team who could build it themselves. They are the businesses where the owner is too busy managing operations to fix the systems. A plumbing company with 12 technicians loses more money from missed follow-ups and manual scheduling than a SaaS company loses from a slow API. The problem is the same. The solution just needs to not require an engineer."

Step 3 — Specificity: "We have built intake and follow-up agents for [relevant industry if known], which means we already understand the common workflows. The friction audit we do is specifically to map your version of those workflows, not to teach you automation theory."

Step 4 — The proof point: "Can I show you a two-minute overview of what a non-tech business in [their industry] is running with us right now? It will make this feel a lot more concrete."

### What to Do If a Deal Is Stuck at Each Stage

**Stuck at Stage 1 (no response after 3 touches):**
Send one final pattern-interrupt message, then move to inactive nurture. Do not continue chasing. "Last message from me on this: I calculated the [specific metric tied to their trigger signal]. If it is not relevant, no action needed. If the timing is better in [month], reply and I will pick this back up."

**Stuck at Stage 2 (friction audit booked but ghost):**
Send a no-meeting version of the friction audit as a written diagnostic. "I put together a quick breakdown of where businesses like yours typically lose time and revenue in [their workflow]. Happy to send it over even if a call does not make sense right now." This provides value without requiring their time and reopens the conversation.

**Stuck at Stage 3 (demo done, no proposal decision):**
Do not chase. Diagnose first. Ask directly: "After the demo, I sensed there might be something that did not land the way I expected. What would need to be different for this to feel like an obvious yes?" This either surfaces the real objection or confirms the deal is not real.

**Stuck at Stage 4 (proposal sent, no response):**
One direct message. Not a nudge. "The proposal has been sitting for [X] days. I want to respect your time, so I am asking directly: is this still something you want to move forward with? Yes, no, or timing is off, all are fine answers. I just need to know." Then wait. If no response in 72 hours, move to closed-lost and note the reason.

## Close Plan Template

A mutual close plan is a shared document or written agreement between you and the prospect that maps the specific steps from where you are now to a signed agreement. It is not a pushy tactic. It is a clarity tool that serious buyers appreciate and unserious buyers avoid.

### The 3 Questions to Ask Before Building the Close Plan

Ask these verbally before the proposal is written. If the prospect cannot answer all three, the deal is not ready for a close plan.

1. "What needs to be true for you to feel confident about moving forward? What are your specific criteria for a yes?"
2. "Who else, if anyone, needs to be involved before you can commit? Is there a business partner, accountant, or team member whose input you need?"
3. "If everything looks right, what is your ideal start date? Is there a deadline that makes earlier better?"

---

### What a Good Close Plan Looks Like for a 14-Day Deal Cycle

| Day | Action | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| Day 0 | Demo completed, verbal interest confirmed | Both |
| Day 1 | Proposal sent with review call invitation | Autoflow |
| Day 2 | Proposal review call (30 min) | Both |
| Day 3 | Any revisions or clarifications sent | Autoflow |
| Day 5 | Decision deadline confirmed ("by end of day Friday") | Prospect |
| Day 7 | Agreement signed, setup fee paid | Prospect |
| Day 10 | Kickoff call scheduled | Autoflow |
| Day 14 | Build begins | Autoflow |

If the prospect agrees to this plan, they have implicitly committed to each step. If they push back on Day 5 or Day 7, you have surfaced an objection that would have otherwise killed the deal silently.

### How to Introduce the Close Plan Without Pressure

Script: "I want to make sure this is as simple as possible for you. A lot of our clients find it helpful when I map out the exact steps from here to going live. Can I share a quick timeline? You tell me if any step does not work for your schedule and we will adjust it."

This frames the close plan as a service to the prospect, not a sales tactic. It also puts the timeline in their hands, which increases ownership.

## Common Deal Killers and Fixes

### Stage 1 Deal Killers

| # | Deal Killer | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Generic outreach with no specific trigger | Identify one specific signal (job post, review, news) and lead with it in the first line |
| 2 | Following up more than 3 times without a new angle | Change the channel or change the message; same message repeated is spam |
| 3 | Calling too early before any context is established | Send the first personalized email before calling; warm the line first |
| 4 | Over-explaining the product in the first message | First message should create curiosity, not deliver a product overview |
| 5 | Reaching out to someone who is not the owner | Verify the contact is the economic buyer before investing in outreach |

---

### Stage 2 Deal Killers

| # | Deal Killer | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Discovery call was all pitch, no questions | Spend the first 20 minutes asking questions before showing anything |
| 2 | Skipping the quantification step | Always calculate the cost of the problem together on the call |
| 3 | Not asking about other stakeholders | Ask "who else is part of this decision?" before the call ends |
| 4 | Not following up with a written call summary | Send a 5-bullet summary within 24 hours; this builds trust and surfaces mismatches |
| 5 | Scheduling a demo before the score hits 50 | Run through the MEDDPICC scorecard before booking the demo; a bad demo wastes both parties' time |

---

### Stage 3 Deal Killers

| # | Deal Killer | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Generic demo not tailored to their industry or workflow | Build the demo using their actual workflow description from the friction audit |
| 2 | Failing to address the competition before the close | Ask "what are you comparing this to?" and address it directly during the demo |
| 3 | Ending the call without a next step on the calendar | Before the demo call ends, confirm the proposal review date and time |
| 4 | Skipping the ROI calculation during the demo | Run the numbers live using their figures; this is the highest-value moment in the entire sales process |
| 5 | Not asking for a verbal commitment at the end of the demo | Ask: "On a scale of 1 to 10, how confident are you that this solves the problem?" Below 7 means you have unresolved objections |

---

### Stage 4 Deal Killers

| # | Deal Killer | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sending the proposal without scheduling the review call first | Always book the review call before sending; say "I will send this over an hour before our call on Thursday" |
| 2 | Proposal has too much generic boilerplate | Every proposal section should reference a specific detail from the discovery call |
| 3 | No urgency mechanism in the proposal | Add a specific start date, capacity note, or limited availability element |
| 4 | Chasing with multiple soft follow-ups | After the review call, send one direct follow-up and wait; multiple soft nudges signal desperation |
| 5 | Surprise stakeholder appears at proposal stage | Prevented at Stage 2 by asking "who else is part of this decision?" — if it happens at Stage 4, schedule a second review call with the new stakeholder before negotiating |

### Stage 5 Deal Killers

| # | Deal Killer | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Agreement sent but not signed within 72 hours | Follow up with a docusign reminder and a brief message: "Reminder, agreement is waiting for your signature. Kickoff slot is held through Friday." |
| 2 | Setup fee invoice goes unpaid without follow-up | Automate a payment reminder sequence; do not start build work until payment is confirmed |
| 3 | Kickoff call not scheduled before close | Confirm kickoff date in the same message as the agreement send; do not leave it as a post-signature to-do |

| 5 | Closed-lost deal not documented with reason | Every lost deal must be logged with a specific reason; patterns across 10 deals will reveal a fixable problem in your process |
