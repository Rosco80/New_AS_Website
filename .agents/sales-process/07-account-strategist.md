# Autoflow Solutions: Post-Sale Account Management & Expansion Playbook

---

## SECTION 1: CLIENT SUCCESS JOURNEY MAP

### The 7-Milestone Arc: Day 1 Through Month 12

---

### MILESTONE 1 — Day 1: Contract Signed

**Operational Delivery**
- Send welcome email within 2 hours of payment clearing (not a template — personalized to their specific engine and business name)
- Book onboarding call within 48 hours
- Send pre-onboarding questionnaire: current tools, WhatsApp number/phone lines, CRM access credentials, team contacts who will interact with the system
- Create client folder: intake form, contract, onboarding checklist, deployment tracker
- Assign client to a named delivery contact (even if that is the founder)

**Commercial (AE/Founder)**
- Send a personal video message (60 seconds, phone camera is fine): "Here is exactly what happens this week and what you can expect in 30 days."
- Confirm the primary success metric discussed in discovery — write it down and share it back to the client in writing. Example: "You told me you want to stop missing reservation calls on weekends. That is what we are building toward."
- Log the client's stated pain, stated goal, and any secondary pain mentioned casually in discovery. The secondary pain is your future expansion signal.

**Metric to Track**
- Onboarding call booked within 48 hours: Yes/No
- Pre-onboarding questionnaire returned within 72 hours: Yes/No

**Risk Flag if Missed**
- If onboarding call is not booked within 72 hours, client is already drifting. Call directly — do not email. A client who slows down before deployment has not yet committed psychologically. This is the earliest possible churn signal and it is almost always addressable with a direct human call.

---

### MILESTONE 2 — Day 7: System Live

**Operational Delivery**
- Engine deployed and active (not in testing — live and customer-facing)
- First real interaction processed by the system (not simulated)
- Delivery team sends a "Day 1 Live" notification to the client with screenshot or log evidence that the first real conversation, booking, or workflow was handled by the system
- Internal QA check: response accuracy, routing logic, handoff behavior

**Commercial (AE/Founder)**
- Check in personally: "The system went live. How does it feel seeing it work?" This is emotional confirmation, not a data call.
- Frame what the next 3 weeks look like: "For the next 30 days, we watch it closely together. I will send you a summary at Day 30 showing exactly what it has handled."
- Do not pitch anything. Do not mention other engines. This call is entirely about their confidence.

**Metric to Track**
- System live by Day 7: Yes/No
- First live interaction logged: Yes/No
- Client sentiment on check-in call: Positive / Neutral / Concerned

**Risk Flag if Missed**
- Deployment delayed past Day 10: escalate internally immediately. A client who waits more than 10 days for their system to go live loses confidence rapidly and begins regretting the purchase. If delay is unavoidable, call the client before they call you. Explain the reason, give a firm new date, and offer a one-week credit proactively. The credit costs less than a churn.

---

### MILESTONE 3 — Day 30: First Value Proof

**Operational Delivery**
- Compile the Day 30 Report:
  - Total interactions handled by the system
  - Estimated hours saved (calculate using average time per task × volume)
  - Error rate / escalation rate (how often the system had to hand off to a human)
  - Any gaps identified and what was adjusted
- Send report 48 hours before the review call so the client can read it

**Commercial (AE/Founder)**
- Run the Day 30 Value Review (20 minutes):
  - Open with their numbers: "In the first 30 days, your system handled X interactions. Based on your team spending roughly Y minutes on each, that is Z hours returned to you."
  - Ask: "Does that match what you have felt on the ground?"
  - Ask: "What has surprised you — positively or negatively?"
  - Listen for a second pain. Do not prompt it. If they say "Actually, my phone calls are still chaos" — note it, do not pitch yet.
- Confirm their continued commitment: "Are we tracking toward what you were hoping for?"

**Metric to Track**
- Day 30 report delivered on time: Yes/No
- Client satisfaction score (ask directly on a 1-10 scale): Target 7+
- Hours saved quantified and documented: Yes/No
- Secondary pain mentioned: Yes/No (log in CRM even if not actioned)

**Risk Flag if Missed**
- If client rates satisfaction below 7, or if the system has handled fewer than 50% of projected interactions, classify account as Yellow immediately. Do not wait. Initiate a stabilization review within 5 business days.
- If the client is non-responsive to the Day 30 call scheduling, that is a red signal. Two missed rescheduling attempts = account moves to Yellow regardless of other indicators.

---

### MILESTONE 4 — Day 60: Habit Formed

**Operational Delivery**
- System has now processed enough volume to identify patterns: peak hours, failure points, most common queries
- Delivery team sends an optimization note: "Based on 60 days of data, we made these 2 adjustments to improve performance."
- This demonstrates ongoing value, not just installation value.

**Commercial (AE/Founder)**
- This is the first genuine expansion check-in — not a pitch, a diagnostic.
- Ask: "Now that the [Engagement/Operations] Engine has been running for two months, what is the next biggest thing still on your plate that takes too much time or costs you too much?"
- If they name something the second engine addresses: "Interesting — we actually see that pattern a lot. That is what [Operations/Engagement] Engine is designed for. Do you want me to show you what that would look like for your business?"
- If they say nothing yet: "Good to know. I will check back in at 90 days. By then you will have a clearer picture."
- No pressure. No proposal yet unless they lean in.

**Metric to Track**
- 3 or more distinct workflows handled by the current engine: Yes/No
- Expansion trigger identified: Yes/No
- Expansion conversation stage logged in CRM: Identified (20%) if applicable

**Risk Flag if Missed**
- Account at Day 60 with fewer than 3 workflows active is underdeployed. Underdeployed accounts churn. Initiate a workflow expansion session with the delivery team — not to sell, but to make sure they are using what they paid for.
- Silence from the client between Day 30 and Day 60 (5+ days of no response to any touchpoint) = Yellow flag. Reach out with a value touchpoint, not an ask: "We spotted something in your data you should see."

---

### MILESTONE 5 — Day 90: Value Locked / Expansion Ready

**Operational Delivery**
- Full 90-day performance report prepared
- System has been through at least one optimization cycle
- Client has experienced at least one moment where the system clearly saved them from a crisis (a call they would have missed, a booking that would have been lost, an admin task completed while they slept)

**Commercial (AE/Founder)**
- Run the first formal QBR (see Section 3 for the full structure)
- If the account is Green and expansion has been identified: introduce the specific engine (see Section 4 for engine-by-engine plays)
- If the account is Yellow: run a stabilization conversation before any expansion. Do not mention additional engines.
- If the account is Red: run a save play (see Section 5). No expansion discussion whatsoever.
- Month 3-4 is the peak churn window. The QBR is your primary defense.

**Metric to Track**
- QBR completed: Yes/No
- ROI documented and shared: Yes/No
- Account health classification: Red/Yellow/Green
- Expansion proposal delivered (if Green): Yes/No

**Risk Flag if Missed**
- If the QBR does not happen at Day 90, rebook within 10 days. Do not let it slip to Month 5. The window between Month 3 and Month 4 is when the novelty has worn off and ROI has not yet become deeply embedded. Missing the QBR in this window is the single most common precursor to churn in subscription service businesses.

---

### MILESTONE 6 — Month 6: Dependency Established

**Operational Delivery**
- System is now embedded in daily operations — staff depend on it, workflows route through it automatically
- Compile 6-month impact summary: cumulative hours saved, interactions handled, measurable business outcomes (bookings, leads captured, calls answered)
- Identify which workflows have never been used or are underperforming

**Commercial (AE/Founder)**
- Run Month 6 QBR with a strategic framing shift: "You are now in a different position than when you started. Let me show you what has changed."
- Reinforce dependency: "Imagine removing this system on Monday. What breaks?" (This is the stickiness test — ask it rhetorically, let them answer in their own head.)
- If second engine has already been sold: assess its performance and begin introducing the third engine concept as a future possibility. No formal proposal yet — just plant the seed.
- If second engine has not yet been sold and account is Green: run the expansion play directly. Six months in with a happy client who has not expanded is a missed NRR opportunity.
- If the client has referred someone: acknowledge it visibly and personally. This deepens loyalty faster than any feature.

**Metric to Track**
- MRR at Month 6 vs. Month 1 (expansion revenue tracked)
- Number of engines active: target 2 for a healthy account
- NPS or direct satisfaction rating: target 8+
- Referrals generated to date: track per client

**Risk Flag if Missed**
- A Green account at Month 6 still on one engine is a commercial risk. Either the expansion play has not been run (delivery failure) or the client has been asked and said no (which should be documented with reason). Both scenarios need to be understood and addressed.

---

### MILESTONE 7 — Month 12: Strategic Partnership Review

**Operational Delivery**
- Annual business review report: full-year ROI summary, workflow count, peak usage periods, system uptime, notable outcomes
- Identify any systems or workflows that have grown beyond current engine capacity
- Flag any new pain areas introduced by business growth (new locations, new services, new staff)

**Commercial (AE/Founder)**
- Reframe the relationship: "A year ago you were trying to solve [original pain]. You have solved it. Now the question is where your business goes next and how we stay ahead of that."
- Renewal conversation (if applicable to contract structure): confirm continuation with enthusiasm, not as a retention task
- Introduce the Execution Engine (Digital Workers) as the natural ceiling — the fully autonomous layer for clients running 2-3 engines well
- Ask for the referral if not yet received (see Section 6)
- Discuss pricing only if they raise it — do not preemptively offer discounts on a healthy account

**Metric to Track**
- Annual NRR for this account: target 130%+ (reflecting at least one engine expansion)
- Engines active at Month 12: target 3-4 for a fully developed account
- Referrals generated: target 1 per client per year
- Client tenure risk score: documented and reviewed

**Risk Flag if Missed**
- A client at Month 12 still on one engine, no referral given, and no expansion in pipeline is a stagnant account. Classify Yellow regardless of satisfaction score. Satisfaction without expansion and referral represents a relationship that has not been developed commercially. Re-engage with an annual review framed around their business growth, not the product.

## SECTION 2: ACCOUNT HEALTH SCORING SYSTEM

### The Autoflow Account Health Scorecard

**Instructions:** Score each active client monthly. Use the most recent 30-day data. Total score determines health band. Run this during your first-of-month review before any client calls.

---

### SCORING INPUTS

| Dimension | Indicator | Scoring |
|---|---|---|
| **Usage** | Interactions/workflows processed by system in last 30 days vs. expected baseline | At or above baseline = 3 pts / 50-99% of baseline = 1 pt / Below 50% = 0 pts |
| **Engagement** | Days since last client-initiated contact or positive response | Within 14 days = 3 pts / 15-30 days = 1 pt / 30+ days = 0 pts |
| **Results** | Quantified ROI delivered vs. promised outcome (hours saved, leads captured, calls answered) | On or above target = 3 pts / 60-99% of target = 1 pt / Below 60% = 0 pts |
| **Satisfaction** | Direct satisfaction score from most recent check-in (ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how is this working for you?") | 8-10 = 3 pts / 5-7 = 1 pt / 1-4 or not asked = 0 pts |
| **Escalations** | Number of support tickets or system issues in last 30 days | 0-1 = 3 pts / 2-3 = 1 pt / 4+ = 0 pts |
| **Commercial** | Payment on time, no billing disputes | On time = 2 pts / 1-5 days late = 1 pt / 5+ days late or disputed = 0 pts |
| **Expansion Signal** | Unprompted mention of second pain, referral given, or business growth visible | Strong signal = 2 pts / Weak/indirect signal = 1 pt / No signal = 0 pts |

**Maximum Score: 19 points**

---

### HEALTH BANDS

| Band | Score | Classification | Posture |
|---|---|---|---|
| Green | 14-19 | Healthy / Expanding | Run expansion play |
| Yellow | 8-13 | At Risk / Stabilize | Stabilize before any expansion |
| Red | 0-7 | Danger / Save | Save play only — no expansion whatsoever |

---

### ACTIONS BY HEALTH BAND

**GREEN (14-19 points)**
- Monthly update sent by delivery team
- AE/founder personal check-in call or voice note monthly
- QBR at Month 3, 6, 12 with ROI data prepared
- Expansion play introduced when: score 14+ AND 60+ days post-launch AND 3+ workflows active
- Referral ask when: score 16+ AND a clear value milestone has been reached

**YELLOW (8-13 points)**
- AE/founder personal call within 3 business days of classification
- Stabilization conversation: "I want to make sure this is really working for you. Can we spend 20 minutes reviewing what you are getting from it and what we could improve?"
- Set a 30-day stabilization target: specific changes or improvements with named owner and date
- No expansion conversation until score returns to Green
- Check score again in 30 days — if still Yellow, escalate internally

**RED (0-7 points)**
- AE/founder calls within 24 hours — do not email
- Run save play immediately (see Section 5)
- Delivery team briefed: any open issues given priority queue
- Commercial: consider what low-cost gesture can change the emotional tone (service credit, extra session, founder attention)
- No expansion. No referral ask. No pitching. Only saving.

---

### LEADING INDICATORS OF CHURN (Detect Before the Score Drops)

These are signals to watch independent of the monthly scorecard. If you see any one of these, act that day — do not wait for the next scoring cycle.

1. **The Silent Client:** No response to any touchpoint for 5 or more consecutive business days. Silence in SME accounts is almost never contentment — it is avoidance.

2. **The Rescheduler:** Any review call rescheduled twice without a confirmed new time. Clients who value the relationship reschedule and confirm. Clients who are disengaging or unhappy reschedule into uncertainty.

3. **The Support Spike:** Two or more support tickets in a single week after a period of low volume. A sudden surge in issues often signals a workflow change or staff change that was not communicated to you.

4. **The Billing Hesitation:** An invoice that is 5 or more days late from a client who previously paid on time. Do not wait for finance to flag this. Call it a "quick check-in" and listen to the conversation that follows.

5. **The Positive-Sentiment-But-No-Expansion:** A client at Month 4+ who consistently says "everything is great" but has not expanded, not referred, and not asked about anything new. This is not a sign of contentment — it is a sign of limited perceived relevance. They have stopped thinking about Autoflow in the context of their business growth.

6. **The Staff Change:** A key contact (the person you built the relationship with) leaves or changes roles. This is the most underestimated churn trigger in SME accounts. The new person did not buy the system, does not have context, and may actively want to reverse decisions made by their predecessor. React within 24 hours of learning this.

7. **The Public Frustration:** Any complaint or negative comment about the business in a context where they might mention your system — even indirectly. Monitor their Google reviews and social if you have the bandwidth.

## SECTION 3: QBR DESIGN FOR SME CLIENTS

### The Autoflow 30-Minute QBR

**Philosophy:** This is not a corporate slide deck. It is a structured conversation between two people who are working on the same problem. SME owners respond to honesty, specificity, and respect for their time. The QBR should feel like a trusted advisor meeting, not a vendor check-in.

**Format:** Video call preferred. In-person when geography allows. Phone as a last resort — visual presence matters for the expansion conversation.

**Prep time required:** 45 minutes before the call to compile data and review the account notes.

---

### AGENDA (30 Minutes)

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00 - 2:00 | Opening Frame | Set the tone — this is about their results, not the product |
| 2:00 - 10:00 | ROI Review | Show them what they have gotten — in their language |
| 10:00 - 17:00 | Honest Assessment | What is working, what is not, what changes |
| 17:00 - 24:00 | Forward Look | Surface the next opportunity naturally |
| 24:00 - 30:00 | Mutual Action Plan | Close with specific next steps, owned by both sides |

---

### SECTION-BY-SECTION SCRIPTS AND DATA

**0:00 - 2:00: Opening Frame**

Data to prepare: Nothing yet. This is tone-setting.

Script: "Thanks for making time. I want to keep this useful and honest — not a slide show. I have got about 90 days of data from your system and I want to spend the first part of this showing you exactly what has moved, then I want to hear from you what feels right and what does not. Sound good?"

Why this works: You are establishing that you will be honest. SME owners have been through enough vendor calls to know when they are being managed. Starting with "I want to be honest" disarms the defensive posture they arrived with.

---

**2:00 - 10:00: ROI Review**

Data to prepare:
- Total interactions/workflows handled by the system in the quarter
- Calculated hours saved: (interactions × average minutes per task handled manually) ÷ 60
- Convert hours to cost if you know their team rate — or use a conservative £/$/RM20/hour assumption and state the assumption
- Specific outcomes: leads captured, reservations confirmed, calls answered, invoices processed — whatever is specific to their engine
- One "highlight moment": the single best example from the quarter. A call answered at 2am. A booking confirmed while they were on holiday. A roster updated automatically while the manager slept.

Script: "Let me start with the numbers. In the last [90 days], your Engagement Engine handled [X] conversations. If you or your team were doing that manually, at roughly [Y] minutes each, that is [Z] hours. At [reasonable hourly value], that is [£/$/RM amount] of time returned to you this quarter."

Pause. Let them absorb it.

"The moment I want to highlight specifically is [specific example]. That happened at [time], and without the system, that [booking/call/query] would have been missed."

Then ask: "Does that match what you have experienced? Has it felt like that on the ground?"

Listen carefully to the answer. The gap between what the data shows and what they have felt is where the real account risk or real expansion opportunity lives.

---

**10:00 - 17:00: Honest Assessment**

Data to prepare:
- Escalation rate: what percentage of interactions required human intervention?
- Any workflows that failed, broke, or underperformed
- Any adjustments made by the delivery team and why
- The one thing you would change if you could change anything

Script for the "what is working" section: "Here is what the data says is working well: [specific workflow or volume metric]. This is performing above what we would expect for a system at this stage."

Script for the "what is not working" section — and this part matters: "I also want to be straight with you about one thing that is not where I want it to be. [Specific shortfall — escalation rate too high, a particular workflow underperforming, a response gap at certain hours]. Here is what we are doing about it and here is the timeline."

Do not hide underperformance. SME owners notice when you gloss over it and it destroys trust permanently. Naming it yourself — before they have to bring it up — is the single most powerful trust-building move in post-sale account management.

Then ask: "From your side — what has frustrated you or felt like it should be working better?"

Shut up and listen. Whatever they say next is your most important account intelligence of the quarter.

---

**17:00 - 24:00: Forward Look**

This section only happens if the account is Green. If the account is Yellow, replace this section with a stabilization plan. If Red, this section does not exist — move to the action plan focused entirely on their issues.

For Green accounts:

Script: "Based on where you are now, I want to ask you something. Outside of [current engine], where is the next place in your business that still costs you time or causes you stress?"

Wait for their answer. Do not suggest. Do not complete their sentence.

If they name something addressed by a second engine: "That is interesting. We see that a lot with clients at your stage. [Operations/Engagement/Voice] Engine is actually exactly what is designed for that. Do you want me to sketch out what that would look like for your business?"

If they name something outside your current scope: Make a note, acknowledge it, and be honest: "That might be outside what we currently do, but let me think about it and come back to you." (Then actually come back to them — this is a trust moment.)

If they say "honestly, everything is running fine": "That is great to hear. I want to make sure we are staying ahead of your business as it grows. Are there any new services, new locations, or changes you are planning in the next 6 months that we should be factoring in?" This question opens growth-related expansion without sounding like a pitch.

---

**24:00 - 30:00: Mutual Action Plan**

Script: "Let me summarize what we are walking away with. On our side: [specific action, named person, date]. On your side: [whatever they committed to — sending credentials, introducing a team member, reviewing a workflow]. Does that look right?"

Always end with a named date for the next conversation: "Let me put our next check-in in the calendar now. Does [specific date, 4-6 weeks out] work?"

Send a written summary within 24 hours. Three bullet points. Not a formal document — a WhatsApp message or short email. "Here is what we said we would do: [action 1, action 2, action 3]. Speak in [date]."

The written summary is a commercial artifact — it is proof that you follow through, and it keeps the client's next steps visible to them.

## SECTION 4: EXPANSION PLAYBOOK (ENGINE BY ENGINE)

### Play A: Engagement Engine Client → Add Operations Engine

**Client Profile:** Has 24/7 lead capture and chat running. Responding to leads quickly. The front-end communication is solved. The back-end is still manual.

**Trigger Conditions (ALL should be present, or at minimum 3 of 4):**
- 60+ days post-launch
- Client has mentioned CRM, scheduling, invoicing, or admin as a time drain
- System is handling 3+ distinct conversation workflows
- Account health: Green

**How to Introduce It**

Do not open with a product description. Open with a reflection of their stated pain.

Script — Transition Moment: "You mentioned a few weeks ago that even though the lead capture is working, you are still spending [X hours] a week on [admin task — rescheduling, data entry, follow-ups, invoicing]. I want to show you what happens when we connect the back-end to what the front-end is already capturing."

**Value Bridge Script:**

"Right now, [Engagement Engine] is catching leads and handling conversations at the front door. But once a lead comes in, your team is still manually [entering it into your CRM / scheduling the appointment / sending the confirmation / following up three days later]. Operations Engine automates that entire handoff. Lead comes in through chat, gets auto-tagged in your CRM, appointment gets booked, confirmation goes out, follow-up gets scheduled — all without anyone touching it. The result for clients at your stage is typically 20+ hours a week returned to the team. At your current volume, my estimate for your business would be closer to [X hours]. That is [X hours] a week of admin your team never has to do again."

**Objection to Expect:** "I am not sure we are ready for more automation — it feels like a lot."

**Handle it:**

"I hear that. The reason I am bringing this up now rather than at Month 1 is that you have already done the hard part. Your team trusts the system. The front-end is stable. Adding Operations Engine at this point is not starting over — it is connecting what already exists. The onboarding is significantly lighter than your first engine because the foundation is already in place. In terms of time from you, we are talking about one setup session and then it runs. Would it help to see what the specific workflows would look like before you decide?"

**Pricing Bridge:** "$170/month on top of your current $150. Combined you are at $320/month. Based on the 20+ hours saved per week, you are getting that back at a cost of roughly [£/$/RM X per hour of admin replaced]. For most small businesses, that is a fraction of what they pay any staff member to do those tasks."

---

### Play B: Operations Engine Client → Add Engagement Engine

**Client Profile:** Back-office is running well. CRM sync, scheduling, and admin are automated. But they are still losing leads at the front end — missed calls, slow responses, no 24/7 coverage.

**Trigger Conditions:**
- 60+ days post-launch
- Client mentions missed inquiries, slow lead response, or after-hours queries going unanswered
- Business has a consumer-facing component (restaurant, retail, service bookings)
- Account health: Green

**How to Introduce It**

Script — Value Reflection: "Your operations are now running on autopilot in the back end. The question I want to ask you is: how many people are reaching out to you on WhatsApp or your website at 9pm that you are not getting back to until the next morning? Because we track that gap for clients, and for most businesses at your stage, it is somewhere between 15 and 40% of inbound interest that either goes cold or goes to a competitor before 9am."

**Value Bridge Script:**

"Engagement Engine closes that front-door gap. A lead hits your website or WhatsApp at any hour — immediately gets a response, gets qualified, gets the information they need, and in many cases books or confirms without any human involvement. When your team arrives in the morning, the lead is already warm, already in your system thanks to Operations Engine, and already knows who you are. You are essentially removing the 12-hour window where business that you paid to generate goes quiet."

**Objection to Expect:** "We get most of our clients by word of mouth — I am not sure we need a lead capture system."

**Handle it:**

"That is exactly why this matters more for you, not less. Word-of-mouth leads are your highest-quality leads. They already trust you before they reach out. If they message you at 7pm on a Friday and hear nothing until Monday, they have had 60 hours to talk themselves out of it or find someone else. Engagement Engine does not change where your leads come from — it changes what happens to them the moment they raise their hand. For a referral-driven business, that conversion rate improvement is where the real money is."

---

### Play C: Any Engine Client → Add Voice Engine

**Client Profile:** Runs one or two engines. The phone is still a bottleneck. Staff spend significant time on incoming calls. Calls go unanswered during busy periods or after hours.

**Trigger Conditions:**
- Client has mentioned phone volume, missed calls, or staff distraction from calls
- Business has a consumer-facing phone line (restaurant, clinic, trade business, security firm)
- 60+ days post-launch, Green account
- Optional signal: client mentions a specific moment where a missed call cost them a customer

**How to Introduce It**

This play often starts with a question, not a statement.

Script: "Can I ask you something? On a typical Tuesday, how many times does your phone ring that your team either misses or has to interrupt something important to answer?"

Let them answer. They will almost always give you a real number.

"And of those calls, how many are the same three questions — hours, pricing, appointment availability?"

They will confirm this immediately.

"That is what Voice Engine is built for. It answers the phone in under 3 seconds, handles the standard questions automatically, and only routes to a human when it genuinely needs one. 80% of your call volume — handled. Your team gets their focus back. The people who actually need to speak to a human get through faster because the line is not clogged."

**Value Bridge Script:**

"Let me make this concrete. If your team handles [X calls per day] and roughly 80% are repetitive queries, Voice Engine is handling [0.8X] of those automatically. If each call takes [4-5 minutes] of staff time, that is [calculated hours] per day returned. At [local hourly rate], that is [monthly value] in real staff capacity — for $200 a month."

**Objection to Expect:** "Our clients expect to speak to a real person. I do not want them to feel like they are being fobbed off."

**Handle it:**

"That is the most common concern I hear, and it is worth addressing directly. The clients who call with a simple question — 'Are you open on Saturday?' or 'What is your cancellation policy?' — are not expecting or wanting a relationship conversation. They want the answer in 10 seconds and to get on with their day. Voice Engine gives them that — instantly, every time. The clients who genuinely need to speak to someone still get to a human — faster, actually, because the line is not backed up. What you end up with is more of your human attention going to the clients who actually need it, and less of it going to the calls that are just information lookups."

---

### Play D: Multi-Engine Client → Add Execution Engine (Digital Workers)

**Client Profile:** Running 2-3 engines successfully. Multiple workflows automated across front-end and back-end. The client is operationally mature. They may be experiencing business growth and starting to see new manual bottlenecks or are ready to remove themselves from operational decisions entirely.

**Trigger Conditions:**
- Running 2+ engines for 90+ days
- Visible business growth (new location, new services, staff additions)
- Client has mentioned wanting to step back from daily operations
- Client is entrepreneurially ambitious — they think about growth, not just maintenance
- Account health: Green, score 16+

**How to Introduce It**

This play is strategic, not tactical. It should feel like a conversation about their business vision, not about an upsell.

Script: "You have come a long way from where you started. When we first started working together, [original pain] was the big issue. That is handled now. [Second engine] is running. I want to ask you a different kind of question: where do you want to be in two years, and what operational things are still stopping you from getting there?"

Let them talk. Genuinely listen.

"What you are describing sounds like you want to be working on the business, not in it. That is what Execution Engine is designed for — autonomous digital workers who handle complex multi-step tasks end-to-end. Not just responding or processing — actually executing. Reporting, follow-up sequences, campaign management, compliance tracking, vendor coordination — things that currently require a person to own and initiate. With Execution Engine, you are not automating a task. You are replacing a role."

**Value Bridge Script:**

"At your current scale, you have automated your front door and your back office. The gap that remains is the operational decision-making layer — the things someone has to own, initiate, and follow up on. Execution Engine fills that gap. Clients at this stage typically find it replaces the equivalent of a part-time operations coordinator. At $200/month, that is a very different cost structure than hiring."

**Objection to Expect:** "This sounds complex and I am not sure I have the time to implement something new right now."

**Handle it:**

"That is exactly why we do not introduce this until a client is at your stage. You have already done the hard implementation work. Your systems are connected, your workflows are mapped, your team trusts the automation. Execution Engine layers on top of what already exists — we are not starting from scratch. The implementation is lighter than either of your previous engines because the infrastructure is already there. And the time you invest in setting it up is paid back in the first 30 days."

## SECTION 5: CHURN SAVE PLAYBOOK

**Standing rule:** Identify the churn reason accurately before you design the response. The wrong save play for the wrong churn reason accelerates churn rather than preventing it.

---

### Churn Reason 1: Client Not Seeing Results (System Deployed But ROI Not Felt)

**The Dynamic:** The system is technically working. Workflows are running. But the client does not feel the impact. This happens when the system is solving the wrong problems, the volume is too low to feel meaningful, or the original success metric was never properly defined.

**Early Warning Signs:**
- Satisfaction score drops from 8+ to 6 or below between Month 1 and Month 3
- Client uses vague language in check-ins: "It is fine," "It is okay," "I guess it is working"
- No client-initiated positive feedback in 45+ days
- Client references the manual process they still do, not the one the system has replaced
- They stop mentioning the system in conversation entirely — it has become invisible in a bad way, not a good way

**Save Script:**

Call (do not email): "I have been looking at your account data and I want to be direct with you. The system is processing [X] interactions, but I am not sure that matches what you expected to feel. Can we take 20 minutes to be really honest about whether this is solving the right problem?"

In the call: Do not defend the system. Do not list features. Ask:

"When you signed up, the thing you most wanted to solve was [original pain]. On a scale of 1-10, how solved does that feel right now?"

If the answer is below 7: "That is important feedback and I am glad you are honest with me. I want to understand whether this is a configuration issue — meaning we are automating the wrong things — or whether the problem is different from what we expected. Can you walk me through a typical [day/week] and show me where you are still spending time you did not expect to be spending?"

This conversation almost always reveals one of two things: the system is deployed on low-impact workflows (fix it) or the client's primary pain is not what was discovered in the sale (reframe or rebuild).

**What to Offer If the Relationship Is at Genuine Risk:**

- A workflow rebuild session at no additional cost: "I want to spend one session with you mapping the three highest-impact things we could automate and then rebuild the system around those. No additional charge — this is on us."
- A 30-day performance guarantee with a defined metric: "Let us agree on one specific number — leads captured, hours saved, calls answered — and if we do not hit [X] in 30 days, I will refund that month."
- Do not offer a discount proactively. A discount without fixing the underlying problem buys you 30 days and then loses the client anyway — and you have now set a precedent that your pricing is negotiable under pressure.

---

### Churn Reason 2: Budget Pressure (Client Needs to Cut Costs)

**The Dynamic:** The business is under financial pressure. The client is looking at their subscriptions and trying to reduce outgoings. Autoflow appears on the cut list not necessarily because it is not working, but because it is a relatively new expense without a long history of being "essential."

**Early Warning Signs:**
- Invoice paid late for the first time by a client who was always on time
- Client mentions a difficult month, a slow season, or rising costs in casual conversation
- Client asks, unprompted, how they could "reduce" or "pause" the service
- Business sector is going through a visible market downturn (hospitality, retail, construction)

**Save Script:**

Do not wait for the cancellation email. The moment you see the late payment or hear "difficult month" — call.

"I noticed your invoice was a bit later this month and I wanted to check in — not about the payment, but because I know that sometimes means things are tighter than usual. I would rather have a straight conversation with you about it than have you feel like you have to make a decision without talking to me first."

Then listen. Do not pitch. Do not immediately offer a discount.

If they confirm budget pressure: "I hear you. Let me ask you one thing before we talk about options. If you removed this system on Monday, what would come back? Who would be doing what, and how much would that cost you in time or in wages?"

This question reframes the cost. Instead of "$150/month for software," it becomes "the alternative is paying [person] to do [task] manually, which costs [more]."

If they are still wavering: "Here is what I can do. I would rather keep working with you at a structure that works than lose you completely. Can we look at which workflows are delivering the most value and make sure the system is maximally focused there? And if it would help, I can offer you [one month at 50% / a 2-month pause with guaranteed reactivation] to get through the tight period."

**What to Offer If the Relationship Is at Genuine Risk:**

- A 2-month pause option: they do not pay, the system sits dormant, they reactivate at the same rate. This is better than a cancellation — you retain the client relationship and the likelihood of reactivation is high if you stay in touch during the pause.
- A tier reduction: if they are on two engines, offer to pause the second engine and remain on one. This cuts their cost in half while keeping the relationship and the most valuable engine running.
- Never offer a permanent discount to retain a budget-pressured client — it trains the behavior and establishes a new price anchor. A temporary accommodation is commercially very different.

---

### Churn Reason 3: Client Feels Unsupported (System Changed or Broke and Nobody Helped)

**The Dynamic:** Something went wrong — a workflow failed, the WhatsApp integration broke, a response went to the wrong person, a call was misrouted — and the client did not feel like Autoflow responded appropriately. This is not primarily a technical churn. It is a relationship churn caused by a perceived abandonment at a moment of vulnerability.

**Early Warning Signs:**
- Two or more support tickets in a short window
- Support ticket with an emotional tone: "This is not acceptable," "This needs to be fixed immediately," or simply an irritated voice note
- No response from the client to a delivery team follow-up after a known issue
- Client's staff member (not the primary contact) reaches out directly to complain
- A known system issue that was not proactively communicated to the affected client

**Save Script:**

The founder or AE calls — not the delivery team, not a support ticket response.

"I am calling personally because I know there was an issue with [specific problem] and I want to make sure it has been fully resolved and that I understand the impact it had on your business. Can you tell me what happened from your side?"

Listen to the full account without interrupting or defending.

"That should not have happened. And more importantly, you should have heard from us the moment we knew about it — not after you noticed it yourself. I am sorry that was not the experience you had."

Do not offer excuses. Do not explain the technical cause unless they ask. The emotional repair comes before any technical explanation.

"Here is what we have done to fix it: [specific fix]. Here is what we are doing to make sure it does not happen again: [specific process change]. And here is what I want to offer you for the disruption: [specific gesture — a month credit, a priority check-in, a direct line to you personally]."

Then close the emotional loop: "I want to be straight with you — this relationship matters to me. Is there anything else that has been bothering you that I should know about?"

This question opens the door for any secondary grievance they have been holding. Give them the chance to clear it now. If they hold it, it will surface later at a worse moment.

**What to Offer If the Relationship Is at Genuine Risk:**

- A one-month credit applied to the next invoice — immediate, no paperwork, just say it and do it
- A weekly check-in for the following 30 days from the founder personally — demonstrating that the account has elevated attention
- A "direct line" commitment: "If anything is ever not working, you text me directly. You do not go through a ticket system." This costs nothing and is worth more than any discount.
- Do not offer to cancel workflows or scale back the system in response to a support failure. That signals the system is unreliable, which accelerates the churn conversation rather than preventing it.

## SECTION 6: REFERRAL ACTIVATION SYSTEM

### The Timing Principle

Referrals are emotional gifts. They happen when a client is experiencing peak satisfaction — the moment they have just seen something that made them feel good about their decision. You ask at that moment, not at Month 12 because the calendar says so.

The optimal referral ask moments, in order of effectiveness:
1. The first time the system does something they did not expect — catches a lead at 2am, answers a call while they were in a meeting, saves a booking they would have missed
2. The Day 30 Value Review — when you have just shown them quantified ROI for the first time
3. The first QBR — when the relationship is warm and the results are documented
4. Any moment they proactively say something positive to you ("This thing is amazing" is a referral ask moment)

The ask is never: "Do you know anyone who might be interested?" That is weak and makes them do all the work mentally.

The ask is always: "I am thinking specifically about [type of business] in [their world]. Do you know anyone like that?"

---

### Referral Tracking System

**In your CRM, for each client, track:**
- Date of referral ask
- Client's response: Enthusiastic / Willing / Hesitant / No
- Names or leads provided (even if not yet contacted)
- Status of each referred lead: Contacted / In conversation / Proposed / Closed / Dead
- Reward issued: Yes/No/Pending

**Follow-up protocol after the ask:**
- If a client said yes and gave a name: follow up within 48 hours. Thank the referring client with a personal message (not a template) once the referral conversation has happened, regardless of outcome.
- If a client said maybe: follow up in 30 days with a value touchpoint, not a repeat ask. Let them come to it.
- If a client said no: log it, respect it, and do not ask again for at least 6 months or until a new satisfaction milestone is reached.

---

### Incentive Structure

**What to offer:** One month of complimentary service per converted referral. Applied to their next invoice, no conditions, stated upfront.

**What to avoid:**
- Cash payments: changes the nature of the relationship from professional to transactional
- Percentage commissions: creates a conflict of interest in their mind and makes them feel like an affiliate rather than a satisfied client
- Tiered reward structures: too complex, creates disappointment when expectations diverge
- Rewards contingent on a 6-month contract or other conditions: makes the promise feel conditional and damages trust

The single most effective referral incentive is not the formal reward — it is the moment you personally acknowledge what they did for you. A phone call to say "that introduction you made converted — I really appreciate it" is worth more than a $150 credit in terms of deepening the relationship and making them want to refer again.

## SECTION 7: CLIENT COMMUNICATION CADENCE

### The Standard Communication Rhythm

**Philosophy:** SME owners are not waiting for your newsletter. They are running a business. Every communication from Autoflow should have one of three purposes: demonstrate value, prevent a problem, or advance the relationship. If it serves none of those, do not send it.

---

### Communication Calendar (Per Active Client)

| Frequency | Communication | Owner | Format | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Welcome message + personal video | AE/Founder | WhatsApp or email + video | Trust and excitement |
| Day 7 | "System is live" notification | Delivery Team | WhatsApp or email | Operational confidence |
| Weekly (Weeks 1-4) | Brief check-in or data snippet | AE/Founder | WhatsApp (1-2 sentences) | Establish presence early |
| Monthly | Monthly Performance Update | Delivery Team | Email with short summary | Value demonstration |
| Monthly | Personal check-in | AE/Founder | Call or voice note | Relationship maintenance |
| Quarterly | QBR | AE/Founder | Video call (30 min) | Strategic review + expansion |
| Ad hoc | Proactive issue notification | Delivery Team → AE/Founder | Call first, then email | Trust through transparency |
| Ad hoc | Win notification | Delivery Team | WhatsApp | Emotional reinforcement |

**The Weekly WhatsApp (Weeks 1-4 only):** This is the period of highest anxiety and lowest habit formation. A one-line message referencing something real — "Your system handled 47 conversations this week, including three after midnight" — costs 30 seconds and builds a disproportionate amount of confidence. After Month 1, reduce to monthly personal touch.

**The Win Notification:** Whenever the system does something genuinely impressive, message the client that day. "Your system just handled a reservation at 1:37am that would have been missed. Confirmed booking, confirmation sent, added to your calendar." These messages are the primary drivers of expansion conversations and referral asks. They are also the fastest antidote to Yellow account drift.

---

### Monthly Update Template

**Format:** Short email or a formatted WhatsApp message for mobile-first clients. No slides. No attachments unless specifically requested. Readable in 60 seconds.

---

Subject: Your [Month] Autoflow Update — [Business Name]

Hi [First Name],

Here is your [Month] summary:

**What your system handled:**
- [X] conversations / calls / workflows processed
- [Y] hours saved (based on [Z] minutes average per task)
- [Notable metric: bookings confirmed, leads captured, calls answered]

**What we adjusted this month:**
- [1 specific change made and why — even if minor]

**One highlight:**
- [The single best moment — specific, human, real]

**What to watch next month:**
- [One thing we are monitoring or improving]

Any questions or anything feeling off — just reply here or send me a WhatsApp.

[Name]
Autoflow

---

**Notes on this template:**
- "What we adjusted" is critical — it proves ongoing work, not just installation
- "One highlight" is the emotional anchor — make it specific and ideally time-stamped
- Keep total word count under 150 words
- Personalize the business name, always. "Your system" not "the system."

---

### Handling Complaints and Escalations

**The 3-Response Protocol:**

**Response 1 — Immediate Acknowledgment (within 2 hours of complaint):**
"I have seen this. I am looking into it right now. I will update you by [specific time today]."
- Owner: AE/Founder or Delivery Lead
- Format: Match the channel they used (WhatsApp if they WhatsApped, call if they called)
- Do not attempt to solve it in this message — only acknowledge it. The worst thing you can do in an SME complaint situation is go quiet while you investigate. Acknowledge first, solve second.

**Response 2 — Resolution Update (within 24 hours):**
"Here is what happened, here is what we have fixed, here is what we are doing to prevent it."
- Be specific. Name the cause if you know it. Do not over-explain or over-apologize — that amplifies the perceived severity.
- If the fix is not complete: "Here is where we are and here is when it will be fully resolved: [specific date/time]."

**Response 3 — Relationship Repair (within 48 hours of resolution):**
AE/Founder call: "I wanted to follow up personally after [issue] to make sure everything is running properly and that you are comfortable. Is there anything else I should know?"
- This call is not about the issue — the issue is resolved. It is about the relationship. The client should leave the call feeling like a person who matters, not a ticket that was closed.

**Escalation Trigger:** Any complaint where the client uses language indicating emotional frustration (not just operational frustration), any issue that caused visible business harm (a missed booking, an unanswered call during a peak period), or any complaint from a client who is already Yellow or Red on the health scorecard — escalate to the founder personally, regardless of the delivery team's assessment.

**What Not to Do:**
- Do not auto-respond with "We have received your ticket." Small business owners are not enterprise IT clients. That response tells them they are being managed, not served.
- Do not make the client repeat the problem to multiple people. Whoever takes the first contact owns it through to resolution.
- Do not offer a credit proactively in the first response. It signals guilt before you have assessed the situation. Offer it in Response 3 after the relationship has been repaired, as a gesture — not a settlement.
