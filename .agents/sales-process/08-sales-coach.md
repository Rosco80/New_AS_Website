# Autoflow Solutions — Complete Sales Coaching System

---

## SECTION 1: REP SKILL ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORK

### How to Use This Framework
Score each competency 1–3. Run this assessment at hire, at 60 days, and quarterly. Any score of 1 after 90 days is a coaching priority. Two or more scores of 1 after 6 months is a management conversation, not a coaching conversation.

---

### Competency 1: Outbound Sequencing

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | Sends generic "just checking in" emails. No signal-based triggers. Same message to all three ICPs. Sequence breaks after touch 2 because they run out of things to say. |
| **Proficient (2)** | Uses pain-track language matched to ICP. Completes all 5 touches. References a visible signal (Google review, job post, website). Reply rate above 3%. |
| **Excellent (3)** | Each touch adds new value or a new angle. Uses pattern interrupts on touch 3-4. Personalizes beyond the signal — references something specific about the business. Reply rate above 7%. Tracks which subject lines and hooks generate the most opens by ICP. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
Pull the last 10 outbound sequences from the CRM. Check: Was the ICP identified before the message was written? Does the message reference a specific signal? Does the pain track match the ICP category? Count what percentage of sequences reached touch 5. Pull reply rate by rep.

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"When you wrote that first message, what specific signal told you this person was in pain right now — and if you didn't have one, why did you send it?"

---

### Competency 2: Discovery and Qualification

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | Asks 2-3 surface questions then pivots to pitch. Discovery lasts less than 10 minutes. Talks more than the prospect in the first half of the call. Qualifies on budget, not on pain. |
| **Proficient (2)** | Runs a structured Friction Audit. Hits all 3 friction categories. Talk ratio is 40/60 or better (rep/prospect) during discovery. Has an upfront contract at the start. |
| **Excellent (3)** | Uses silence effectively after questions. Follows the emotion, not the script — pivots when a prospect reveals unexpected pain. Uncovers a pain the prospect didn't come in expecting to talk about. Has a clear, documented "so what" for each pain revealed. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
Listen to the first 20 minutes of a recorded discovery call. Time how long before the rep mentions their solution. Count unique discovery questions asked. Count how many times the prospect says "yeah, exactly" or similar affirmations — a proxy for feeling heard. Check CRM notes: are pain points documented in the prospect's own words?

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"At what point on that call did you know you had enough to pitch — and how did you know the prospect agreed with that assessment?"

---

### Competency 3: ROI Quantification

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | Never arrives at a dollar figure during discovery. Uses vague language: "save you time," "help you grow." Cannot connect a specific pain to a specific financial outcome. |
| **Proficient (2)** | Walks prospect through time cost calculation for at least one friction point. Gets a number the prospect agrees with. Documents it in the proposal. |
| **Excellent (3)** | Quantifies two or more friction points. Compares the cost of inaction to the cost of the solution in real numbers. Gets the prospect to calculate the number themselves ("so what would you say that's costing you per month?"). Uses the number as the anchor for pricing discussion. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
Pull the last 5 proposals submitted. Does each proposal contain a line-item ROI estimate with numbers the prospect gave? Listen for the "ROI moment" on recordings — is there a specific exchange where a dollar figure is arrived at together? Check win/loss: what was the average documented ROI in won deals vs. lost deals?

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"When this prospect agreed your solution would save them time — whose numbers were those, yours or theirs, and what's the difference in how they'll hold up under a buying committee or a second-thought moment?"

---

### Competency 4: Demo Delivery

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | Gives a feature tour. Shows all three engines regardless of what was surfaced in discovery. Demo is the same for every prospect. No narration connecting the demo to the prospect's specific pain. |
| **Proficient (2)** | Leads with the engine most relevant to the dominant pain. Narrates each demo with a "before" and "after" framing. Pauses after each engine to check for resonance. |
| **Excellent (3)** | Refers to specific things the prospect said in discovery during the demo ("you mentioned your team spends three hours a week on appointment reminders — here's what that looks like automated"). Uses the prospect's own language. Ends each engine demo with a question that surfaces the prospect imagining themselves using it. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
Watch a recorded demo. Count how many times the rep references something the prospect said in discovery. Check whether the demo order matches the priority pain sequence. Ask the rep after the call: "Which engine did you lead with and why?" The answer tells you whether the demo was structured or instinctive.

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"If I watched your demo without having heard the discovery call first, would I be able to tell which prospect you were pitching to — and if not, what does that tell you?"

---

### Competency 5: Proposal Call Execution

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | Emails proposal and waits. Does not book a walkthrough call. If they do get the prospect on a call, reads the proposal to them. Does not ask for a decision at the end. |
| **Proficient (2)** | Books the walkthrough before sending the proposal. Walks through win themes and ROI before price. Handles one or two objections. Attempts to close at the end. |
| **Excellent (3)** | Opens the walkthrough by restating the prospect's own pain back to them. Uses the proposal as a conversation, not a presentation. After presenting price, stays silent. Has a clear close question prepared. Leaves the call with either a yes, a no, or a specific next step with a date — never a "we'll think about it" without a follow-up commitment. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
CRM data: what percentage of proposals were followed by a scheduled walkthrough call within 24 hours? Pull win rate for proposals with a walkthrough call vs. without — this data alone will change behavior. Listen to the final 5 minutes of proposal recordings: does the rep ask for the decision?

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"What specifically did you say to end that proposal call — and if the prospect said 'we'll think about it,' what did you say next?"

---

### Competency 6: Objection Handling

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | Either folds immediately ("totally understand, no problem") or fights the objection ("but our service is really great"). Hears "too expensive" and offers a discount. Treats every objection as a deal-ender. |
| **Proficient (2)** | Acknowledges before responding. Asks a clarifying question to understand the real objection. Has a prepared response to the three most common objections (price, timing, DIY). |
| **Excellent (3)** | Recognizes objection patterns and uses them as diagnostic data. Understands that a price objection late in the process usually means a trust or value gap that was never resolved. Handles "I want to try it myself" with curiosity, not persuasion. Can role-play all 5 common Autoflow objections without breaking stride. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
Note the first objection on a recorded call and then observe the next two minutes. Did the rep ask a question or make a statement? Did the prospect seem more or less engaged after the response? Role-play "it's too expensive" with the rep cold — no preparation — and watch what comes out naturally.

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"When they said it was too expensive, what did you think they were really telling you — and what would have had to be different earlier in the call for that objection not to appear at all?"

---

### Competency 7: Close Discipline

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | Never explicitly asks for the business. Ends calls with "so just let me know." Interprets silence as positive. Follows up with "just checking in" emails. No close date set for any deal in pipeline. |
| **Proficient (2)** | Has a close question prepared and uses it. Sets a specific follow-up date with a reason ("I'll hold this start date for you until Friday"). Deals in Stage 4 have a close date that is within 10 days. |
| **Excellent (3)** | Secures a micro-commitment at every interaction. Uses the prospect's own stated urgency as the reason to move. Comfortable with silence after asking for the decision. Never leaves a deal in Stage 4 past 10 days without either a commitment or a deliberate decision to disqualify. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
Pull all Stage 4 deals from the last 90 days. What is the average number of days between entering Stage 4 and either close or disqualification? How many deals sat in Stage 4 for more than 14 days? Percentage of proposal calls that ended with a decision or a specific follow-up date.

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"What is the specific thing you are waiting for before you ask for the decision — and whose timeline is actually driving that, yours or the prospect's?"

---

### Competency 8: Pipeline Hygiene

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Developing (1)** | CRM is updated inconsistently. Deal stages don't reflect real status. Close dates are not set or are copied forward without thought. Notes are sparse. Can't explain why a deal is in a given stage without looking at emails. |
| **Proficient (2)** | CRM updated within 24 hours of every interaction. Each deal has a next action with a date. Notes include key pain points in the prospect's own words. Stage movement is based on defined criteria, not feeling. |
| **Excellent (3)** | CRM is a live document — can run a pipeline review off CRM alone without supplementing with memory. Can produce a weighted forecast from the pipeline in under 5 minutes. Proactively flags stalled deals before being asked. Pipeline review conversations are substantive because the data is clean. |

**How to Observe/Test:**
Pick 5 deals from the CRM and ask the rep to tell you the story of each one using only what's in the CRM. Any gaps in the story reveal gaps in the record. Count deals with no next action date. Count deals that have been in the current stage for more than 14 days with no update note.

**Coaching Question to Unlock Improvement:**
"If you were hit by a bus today and someone else had to take over your pipeline using only what's in the CRM, which deal would they lose first — and why?"

## SECTION 3: PIPELINE REVIEW COACHING QUESTIONS

**Principle:** Every question should make the rep defend an assumption, not report a fact. If a rep can answer a question by reading from the CRM, the question is not good enough.

---

### Stage 2 — Discovery Complete, Demo Pending (3 Questions)

**Q1: "What is the single most important thing this prospect told you during the Friction Audit — and what specifically makes you believe they told you the truth?"**

This forces the rep to separate what was said from what was felt. It surfaces whether the discovery was genuine or performative. A rep who can't answer with something specific and emotionally resonant likely didn't do real discovery.

**Q2: "If this prospect decides not to buy, what is the most likely reason — and what did you do in discovery to reduce the probability of that outcome?"**

This preemptively surfaces the deal risk the rep already knows about but hasn't addressed. Most reps know exactly where the weakness is. This question makes them say it out loud and own it.

**Q3: "What is this prospect comparing us to — and have you had that conversation directly with them?"**

Reps in Stage 2 almost always avoid competitive conversations because they feel risky. This question forces the rep to surface what the real competition is: DIY, hiring, another vendor, or doing nothing. If the rep doesn't know, the deal has a hidden landmine.

---

### Stage 3 — Demo Complete, Proposal Pending (3 Questions)

**Q4: "At the end of the demo, what did the prospect say that made you believe they would open the proposal?"**

This separates polite positive signals ("that was interesting") from genuine buying intent ("how quickly could this be up and running?"). If the rep can't cite a specific statement, the positive read may be wishful.

**Q5: "What dollar figure did this prospect agree their current problem is costing them — and whose math was that?"**

If the rep says "we didn't get that specific" or "I gave them an estimate," the ROI moment was skipped. Without an agreed number, the proposal's price will land without context. This question exposes that gap before it kills the deal.

**Q6: "Have you confirmed that the person receiving this proposal is the person who writes the check — and how do you know?"**

Many SME deals stall at Stage 3 because the proposal goes to someone who then has to convince someone else. This question surfaces unchecked decision-maker assumptions before the proposal is submitted.

---

### Stage 4 — Proposal Submitted, Decision Pending (3 Questions)

**Q7: "What specific commitment did you get from this prospect at the end of the proposal call — not what you hope they'll do, but what they said they would do?"**

If the rep says "they said they'd think about it," the deal has no close path. This question distinguishes a deal that has a next step from a deal that has hope. Hope is not a stage.

**Q8: "It's been [X] days since the proposal was submitted. What has happened to the prospect's problem in those [X] days — and have you made them feel that?"**

This is the cost-of-inaction question. Deals stall in Stage 4 because the rep stops creating urgency after the proposal is sent. This question forces the rep to reconnect the delay to the ongoing cost of the prospect's friction.

**Q9: "If this deal doesn't close by your stated close date, what's the most likely reason — and what are you doing about that right now?"**

This is a self-audit question. Reps who have a clear answer are managing the deal. Reps who say "I think it'll close" are not. It surfaces whether the close date is based on real information or was set because the CRM required one.

---

### Stuck Deals — Any Stage (3 Questions)

**Q10: "What does this prospect's world look like if they do nothing — and does the prospect know that you know that?"**

Stuck deals are usually stuck because the pain was surfaced but not sharpened. If the rep can vividly describe the cost of inaction, and if that description resonates with the prospect, deals unstick. If the rep can't describe it vividly, the discovery work wasn't deep enough.

**Q11: "What would have to be true for this deal to close in the next 7 days — and which of those things is within your control?"**

This question separates the rep's sphere of influence from the variables that belong to the prospect. It usually reveals that the rep is waiting for something they could actually go and create. A rep who says "nothing, it's in their hands" has abdicated the deal.

**Q12: "Have you given this prospect a reason — or a deadline — to decide that isn't about your quota?"**

Creating urgency based on the rep's needs ("I want to close this by end of month") is transparent and ineffective. Creating urgency based on the prospect's interests ("if we start this month, you'll have the system live before your peak season") is compelling. This question distinguishes the two.

## SECTION 4: DEAL COACHING FRAMEWORK — "FEELS CLOSE BUT ISN'T MOVING"

When a rep brings you a deal and says some version of "I think they're interested, I just haven't heard back," run this five-question diagnostic before suggesting any action. Each question is designed to surface a specific failure pattern.

---

### Question 1: "What specifically did the prospect say or do that tells you this deal is alive?"

**Failure Pattern It Surfaces:** Happy ears — the rep is treating neutral politeness as a buying signal. "They said it was interesting" is not a buying signal. "They asked about the implementation timeline" is.

**What a Good Answer Sounds Like:** The rep cites a specific behavioral signal (they responded to the proposal within 24 hours, they asked about the onboarding process, they mentioned a specific date they wanted to be live by).

**What a Concerning Answer Sounds Like:** "They seemed really engaged on the call" or "they didn't say no." These are absence-of-rejection signals, not presence-of-interest signals.

**Corrective Action:** Help the rep build a signal taxonomy — the difference between a genuine buying signal, a politeness signal, and a silence signal. Have them score the last 5 deals using this taxonomy and see where happy ears was distorting their judgment.

---

### Question 2: "What was the last specific commitment this prospect made — and did they keep it?"

**Failure Pattern It Surfaces:** The rep has been allowing the prospect to make vague, non-binding commitments ("I'll look at it over the weekend") and treating those as real progress. Commitment patterns are predictive. A prospect who misses small commitments will miss the big one.

**What a Good Answer Sounds Like:** "They said they'd send me a decision by Wednesday and they did — they just said they needed another week." That's a prospect who is still engaged, even if slow.

**What a Concerning Answer Sounds Like:** "They said they'd review it last week but I haven't heard anything." This means the rep set no consequence for the broken commitment and the deal is drifting.

**Corrective Action:** Teach the rep to treat every broken micro-commitment as a signal that requires acknowledgment on the next touch. "You mentioned you'd have a chance to look at this by Wednesday — I want to make sure I'm not missing something on your end." Naming the break is better than pretending it didn't happen.

---

### Question 3: "What is the specific obstacle between where this deal is right now and a signed agreement — and is that obstacle real or invented?"

**Failure Pattern It Surfaces:** Reps who don't know why a deal is stuck will invent a reason that feels comfortable: "they're busy," "it's end of quarter for them," "they're evaluating options." These are cover stories for a deal that may already be lost. The real obstacle is usually one of four things: unresolved price concern, undisclosed second decision-maker, unquantified ROI, or loss of trust.

**What a Good Answer Sounds Like:** "I think the real issue is that the price is a stretch for them and we haven't had a direct conversation about it."

**What a Concerning Answer Sounds Like:** "I'm not really sure — they just seem busy."

**Corrective Action:** Have the rep draft a "direct honesty" email or call script that names the obstacle explicitly: "I want to be direct — most deals that go quiet at this stage have a specific concern driving it. I'd rather understand what it is and work through it together than leave you hanging. What would be most helpful right now?" This often unsticks deals or kills them cleanly, which is equally valuable.

---

### Question 4: "If this prospect decides not to buy, what do you think the real reason will be — and when did you first know that was a risk?"

**Failure Pattern It Surfaces:** Reps almost always know the deal risk early and ignore it. This question makes them say out loud the thing they've been hoping would resolve itself. It also surfaces whether the risk was addressable and the rep just didn't address it — which is a coaching opportunity — versus a fundamental disqualification that should have been caught earlier.

**What a Good Answer Sounds Like:** "Honestly, I've been worried about the price since the beginning. I should have done a better job anchoring the ROI during discovery."

**What a Concerning Answer Sounds Like:** "I don't think it'll fall through" or "I'm really not sure." Either the rep is in denial or they genuinely didn't build enough understanding of the prospect's decision criteria to know what the risk is.

**Corrective Action:** Introduce the deal risk rating into the rep's standard pipeline update practice. Every deal in Stage 3 or 4 should have a documented primary deal risk and a documented mitigation action. This forces the rep to confront the risk rather than carry it quietly.

---

### Question 5: "What is the next thing you are going to do — specifically, with specific words — and what outcome are you expecting from that action?"

**Failure Pattern It Surfaces:** Reps with stuck deals often revert to passive follow-up ("I'll send a follow-up email") without any specificity about what the email will say, what it will ask for, or what result they are trying to create. This question separates a rep with a plan from a rep who is hoping.

**What a Good Answer Sounds Like:** "I'm going to call them Thursday at 10am, and I'm going to say: 'I want to check in on where your head is — is there something we haven't addressed that's making this feel uncertain?' I want to hear whether there's a real obstacle or whether they've quietly moved on."

**What a Concerning Answer Sounds Like:** "I'm going to follow up and see where they're at."

**Corrective Action:** Require that every stuck-deal action item include: (1) the specific channel and time, (2) the specific question or statement to be made, and (3) the two possible outcomes and what happens next in each case. This turns a vague intention into a close plan.

## SECTION 5: COMMON REP FAILURE PATTERNS AND FIXES

---

### Failure 1: Pitching Before Discovery Is Complete

**Specific Observable Behavior:** On call recordings, the rep begins describing the Autoflow solution, specific engines, or pricing before the 20-minute mark of the call. Discovery questions stop once the rep senses a pain and they immediately pivot to "let me show you what we do."

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow:** Autoflow sells to SME owners who are perceptive about being sold to. An early pivot to pitch signals that the rep was never really listening — they were waiting for permission to pitch. It also means the ROI moment is skipped, so the price in the eventual proposal lands with no context. The prospect hasn't computed the cost of their own problem, so the price feels like a cost rather than an exchange.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "At what exact minute on that call did you start talking about Autoflow — and how did you know the prospect was ready for that conversation?"
- Exercise: Record a role-play discovery call where the rep is not allowed to mention the solution, the company name, or any feature for the first 20 minutes. At 20 minutes, the rep must summarize what they've learned before moving on. Debrief: what additional pain did they uncover by staying in discovery longer?

**How to Measure Improvement:** Track the average "pitch initiation timestamp" across recorded calls. Target: no pitch language before minute 18–20. Within 30 days of coaching, 80% of calls should show pitch initiation after minute 18.

---

### Failure 2: Not Quantifying ROI During the Friction Audit

**Specific Observable Behavior:** Review of last 10 proposals shows no agreed dollar figure in the ROI section. On recorded calls, the rep uses phrases like "save you a lot of time" or "significant cost reduction" without arriving at a number with the prospect. Proposals are submitted with generic ROI language rather than numbers from the call.

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow:** Without a prospect-generated ROI number, pricing is pure cost. The prospect has no frame for evaluating whether $1,349/month is expensive or cheap. When they review the proposal alone (or with a partner), they have nothing to compare the investment against except their gut feeling about the price. The ROI moment is the only tool the rep has to transform a price objection into a value conversation — and if it never happened, there is no tool.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "When you submitted this proposal without a specific ROI number in it, what did you expect the prospect to think when they looked at the price?"
- Exercise: Practice the "time audit calculation" out loud until it is fluent. Script: "Tell me — on a typical week, roughly how many hours is your team spending on [friction point]? And what would you say an hour of your time is worth when you're doing that kind of admin work? So in a month, that's about... does that feel right to you?" Rep must role-play this calculation three times with different prospect profiles until it feels natural, not scripted.

**How to Measure Improvement:** Audit the next 5 proposals. Each proposal must contain a specific dollar figure with attribution ("as you shared during our call, approximately $X per month"). Set a 4-week target: 100% of proposals contain a prospect-confirmed ROI number.

---

### Failure 3: Emailing Proposals Without Booking a Live Walkthrough

**Specific Observable Behavior:** CRM shows proposal sent via email on a given date with no associated meeting booked. Rep's reason: "I didn't want to seem pushy" or "they said they'd review it and get back to me." Stage 4 deal has no activity for 5+ days after proposal submission.

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow:** A proposal email gets opened once and then competes with 47 other emails for attention. The rep is not present to handle objections, reinforce the ROI frame, or ask for the business. Autoflow's proposal has real depth — the win themes, the ROI summary, the implementation plan — none of which has any impact if the prospect skims it during lunch. The live walkthrough is not a courtesy. It is the most important sales call in the entire cycle.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "When you sent that proposal without a walkthrough booked, what scenario were you imagining would lead to a yes — walk me through it step by step."
- Exercise: Role-play the end of a demo call. The rep must book the walkthrough meeting before ending the call. Script: "I'm going to put the proposal together and I'd rather walk you through it live than just email it over — what does Thursday or Friday look like for 30 minutes?" Rep practices handling every common pushback ("just send it, I'll review it" — "Totally, I'll send it over before we meet so you've seen it. Let's grab 20 minutes Thursday and I can answer any questions as they come up — does 2pm work?").

**How to Measure Improvement:** Track the ratio of proposals submitted to walkthrough calls booked. Baseline it first. Target within 60 days: 80% of proposals have a walkthrough call booked before or immediately after sending. Track win rate for proposals-with-walkthrough vs. proposals-without. Share this data with the rep — it will do more than any coaching conversation.

---

### Failure 4: Happy Ears — Treating "Sounds Interesting" as a Buying Signal

**Specific Observable Behavior:** Pipeline has multiple deals in Stage 3 or Stage 4 where the rep's most recent note reads "they seemed interested" or "good conversation." CRM forecast shows these deals at 70%+ probability. When asked what makes these deals likely to close, rep cites tone and enthusiasm rather than specific behavioral signals or stated intent.

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow:** Happy ears doesn't kill the deal directly — it kills the rep's ability to manage it. Because the rep believes the deal is going well, they don't probe for hidden objections, don't create urgency, and don't ask for the decision. The deal drifts. When it eventually goes quiet, the rep is surprised. Autoflow deals in the $1,349–$4,000 range involve meaningful money for an SME owner. "Interesting" is how they get off the phone politely. Intent sounds different.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "What did this prospect do — not say, do — that tells you they want to buy?"
- Exercise: Build a buying signal inventory together. List the real buying signals in Autoflow deals: asking about implementation timeline, asking about contract terms, asking "what would the first 30 days look like," referencing the solution to a specific upcoming event. Compare these to polite engagement signals. Rep reviews their last 5 deals and retroactively re-forecasts them using behavioral signals only.

**How to Measure Improvement:** Track forecast accuracy: compare rep's probability estimates at time of Stage 4 entry against actual outcomes. Over 90 days, a rep with good happy-ear calibration should have forecast accuracy within 15 percentage points. High happy-ears reps will show consistent over-forecasting of Stage 3 and Stage 4 deals.

---

### Failure 5: Letting Stage 4 Deals Stall Past 10 Days Without Confronting the Delay

**Specific Observable Behavior:** CRM shows deals in Stage 4 with no update activity for 11+ days. Last note says "waiting to hear back." Rep describes these deals as "in progress" in pipeline reviews. No action plan for re-engagement exists in the CRM record.

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow:** Inertia kills SME deals faster than objections. An SME owner is distracted, under-resourced, and making 50 decisions a day. If the rep doesn't create momentum, the prospect's momentum goes to the loudest problem in their life that week — and it won't be the Autoflow proposal. Every day past 10 in Stage 4 without a touch is a day the rep is ceding the deal to entropy.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "What do you think happened to this prospect's problem in the last 11 days — and if the answer is 'it got worse,' what are you doing about that?"
- Exercise: Write the re-engagement message together in the coaching session. Not "just checking in." Something like: "I know you've had a week since you last looked at this — I also know the [specific problem they mentioned] hasn't stopped costing you time. I'd rather find out where your head is now than assume. Worth a 10-minute call this week?" Role-play delivering this as a call, not just a message.

**How to Measure Improvement:** Establish a 10-day Stage 4 rule: any deal with no activity in Stage 4 after 10 days triggers a mandatory rep action. Track pipeline aging weekly. Target: zero Stage 4 deals with 11+ days of inactivity. Within 60 days, this metric should show consistent improvement.

---

### Failure 6: Not Asking for the Decision at the End of the Proposal Call

**Specific Observable Behavior:** Listen to the final 3 minutes of proposal call recordings. Call ends with rep saying some version of "so take a look and let me know what you think." No explicit close question. No specific next step with a date. CRM note after the call reads "they're reviewing."

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow:** Not asking for the decision is not kindness — it is a miss. The prospect has just sat through a 45-minute proposal. Their emotional state at the end of that call — when the ROI is fresh, when the pain has been restated, when the solution has been made vivid — is the best emotional state they will ever be in relative to this decision. Every day after that call, doubt, distraction, and inertia accumulate. The rep who doesn't close at the end of the proposal call is choosing the worst possible moment to ask for the decision: later, via email, when the prospect is cold.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "What were you afraid would happen if you asked for the decision at the end of that call — and what actually happened because you didn't?"
- Exercise: Role-play the last 3 minutes of a proposal call. The rep must practice three close variants until at least one is comfortable: (1) The direct ask: "Based on everything we've covered today, does this feel like the right fit and the right time to move forward?" (2) The assumption close: "If you're happy with what we've covered, the next step is a simple onboarding form — I can send that over right now while we're on the call." (3) The either/or: "Do you want to get started at the beginning of next month, or would the 15th work better for your schedule?" The rep practices staying silent after the question.

**How to Measure Improvement:** Track the percentage of proposal calls that end with either a yes, a no, or a specific follow-up date with a reason. Target: 90% within 60 days. Track close rate on calls where the close question was asked vs. where it was deferred — share this data with the rep.

---

### Failure 7: Mixing Up Setup Fee and MRR in Forecasting

**Specific Observable Behavior:** Pipeline value in CRM doesn't clearly separate one-time setup fee from monthly recurring revenue. Rep forecasts a "$4,000 deal" when the deal structure is $800 setup + $300/month, overstating near-term revenue. Forecast totals are inconsistent from week to week.

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow (the Business):** This failure pattern doesn't kill individual deals — it kills business decisions. If the founder is making decisions about hiring, marketing spend, or capacity based on a pipeline that conflates setup fees with recurring revenue, they are flying blind. A pipeline of "20 deals at $2,000 each" means something very different if those deals are retainer-heavy vs. setup-heavy. Autoflow's growth model depends on MRR, not one-time fees.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "If every deal in your pipeline closed tomorrow at the value you've listed, what would next month's recurring revenue be — and can you calculate that for me right now?"
- Exercise: Rebuild the pipeline view with two columns: projected setup fee and projected MRR. Rep does this live. In the process of building it, most reps realize they haven't been tracking the split — which is the learning. Going forward, every new deal entered in the CRM must have both fields populated before it moves past Stage 2.

**How to Measure Improvement:** Audit the pipeline monthly. Every deal in Stage 2 or higher must have distinct setup fee and MRR values populated. Track forecast accuracy separately for MRR (monthly target vs. actual monthly new MRR added) — this is the metric that matters for the business.

---

### Failure 8: Never Asking for Referrals From Happy Clients

**Specific Observable Behavior:** Client has been on retainer for 3+ months, regularly responds positively to check-ins, has never been asked for a referral. No referral request in the CRM record. Rep waits for referrals to happen organically and treats them as luck rather than a system.

**Why It Kills Deals for Autoflow:** Autoflow's most cost-effective pipeline is referral pipeline. For a founder-led or small team business, a single referral converts at 3-4x the rate of cold outbound, costs zero in acquisition, and enters the funnel with pre-established trust. Not asking for referrals from happy clients is the equivalent of leaving free deals in the field. In a business with a 2-5 week sales cycle, a single referral per month per active client compounds dramatically.

**Coaching Intervention:**
- Question: "You've spoken to this client four times this month and every conversation has been positive — what are you waiting for before you ask them who else you should know?"
- Exercise: Write the referral ask script together. It should never feel like a favor request — it should feel like an extension of the value conversation: "You've mentioned a couple of times that [result they're experiencing] has changed how you operate. Most people I work with who get to this point know one or two other business owners dealing with similar chaos — would you be comfortable introducing me to someone who you think could benefit from this?" Practice delivering this at the end of a quarterly check-in call.

**How to Measure Improvement:** Track referral asks per client per quarter (CRM activity type: "referral conversation"). Track referral conversion rate. Target: every client who has been on retainer 90+ days has been asked for a referral at least once. Track what percentage of new pipeline per quarter comes from referral vs. outbound. In a healthy Autoflow business, this should trend toward 30-40% referral within 12 months of building the referral habit.

## SECTION 6: 30-60-90 DAY ONBOARDING PLAN FOR FIRST SALES HIRE

**Context:** This plan is designed for a founder who is onboarding their first AE. The founder is still selling. The hire is transitioning from learning to executing to owning within 90 days.

---

### Phase 1: Days 1–30 — Learn the Machine

**What They Learn and Practice:**

In the first 30 days, the hire is a student. They are not calling prospects. They are building the mental model of who Autoflow's customers are, what they care about, what they fear, and why they buy.

- Days 1–5: Product immersion. Live inside all three automation engines as if they are the client. Understand every workflow from the client's perspective, not the technical spec. Deliverable: write a one-page "life without Autoflow" description for each of the three ICPs (wellness, professional services, hospitality).
- Days 6–10: ICP immersion. Read the last 20 win and loss notes in the CRM. Listen to 5 recorded discovery calls. Identify the 3 most common pain statements per ICP in the prospect's own words. Deliverable: ICP pain dictionary — 5 real phrases per ICP that signal buying intent.
- Days 11–20: Process mastery. Learn every stage of the sales process end-to-end. Shadow the founder on 3 live calls (discovery, demo, proposal). Role-play each call type with the founder as the prospect. Deliverable: pass a timed role-play of a full Friction Audit call (30 minutes) with a score of 40+ on the call scorecard.
- Days 21–30: Outbound mechanics. Write 3 complete 5-touch outbound sequences, one per ICP. Understand the signal-based trigger system. Send first 15 outbound messages under founder review. Deliverable: 15 messages sent, reply rate tracked, debrief on what worked.

**Accountabilities:**
- Complete all deliverables listed above on time
- Attend all pipeline reviews as an observer
- Document 3 questions per day in a learning journal — questions the hire doesn't know the answer to yet

**How Success Is Measured:**
- Call scorecard role-play score of 40+ by day 30
- ICP pain dictionary completed and reviewed with founder
- First outbound messages reviewed and approved (not just sent)
- Learning journal reviewed weekly — quality of questions asked is a proxy for quality of attention

**Coaching Touchpoints:**
- Daily: 10-minute end-of-day check-in (first two weeks). What did you learn? What confused you? What do you want to try?
- Weekly: 30-minute structured debrief on the week's observations. Founder plays the role of the coaching question asker, not the answer-giver.
- Day 30: formal mid-phase review. Review call scorecard role-play recording together. Score it together. Identify the two competencies to develop in Phase 2.

---

### Phase 2: Days 31–60 — Earn the Right to the Pipeline

**What They Learn and Practice:**

In Phase 2, the hire starts running real prospect interactions — but under supervision. They do not own a pipeline yet. They are building live reps with a safety net.

- Days 31–40: Shadow-to-lead transition. Founder and hire co-run discovery calls, with the hire leading and the founder available to step in. After each call, the hire scores themselves on the call scorecard before hearing founder feedback. Deliverable: 5 co-run discovery calls completed.
- Days 41–50: Solo discovery with debrief. Hire runs discovery calls solo. Every call is recorded. Founder reviews recording before the weekly coaching session and prepares 3 specific observations. Deliverable: 5 solo discovery calls, average scorecard score tracked.
- Days 51–60: Full cycle exposure. Hire runs a deal from first contact through demo and into proposal stage, with founder reviewing at each stage gate before progression. Deliverable: 2 deals progressed to Stage 3 under founder sign-off.

**Accountabilities:**
- Minimum 10 discovery calls (co-run + solo) in Phase 2
- All calls recorded and logged in CRM with full notes
- Weekly scorecard self-assessment submitted before coaching session
- Outbound: continue sending 15+ targeted outbound messages per week with reply rate tracking

**How Success Is Measured:**
- Average discovery call scorecard score of 42+ by day 60
- CRM hygiene audit: 100% of deals have complete notes and next actions
- Reply rate on outbound messages trending above 4% by week 8
- Two deals in Stage 3 by end of Phase 2

**Coaching Touchpoints:**
- Weekly: 45-minute formal coaching session using the call scorecard. Pick the lowest-scoring competency. Run one role-play to work on it. Set one specific behavioral target for the next week.
- Bi-weekly: pipeline review of all hire's deals. Coach uses Stage 2 and Stage 3 pipeline review questions from Section 3.
- Day 60: formal phase review. Review aggregate scorecard trends. Identify which competencies have improved. Identify the one competency requiring continued development in Phase 3.

---

### Phase 3: Days 61–90 — Own the Number

**What They Learn and Practice:**

In Phase 3, the hire has a quota and owns their pipeline. The founder steps back from co-running calls and into a true coaching role. The hire is accountable for results, not just activity.

- Days 61–75: Full pipeline ownership. Hire runs their own outbound, conducts all discovery and demo calls, and submits proposals. Founder is available for deal strategy conversations but does not participate in calls unless the hire asks. Weekly pipeline review happens with full deal coaching.
- Days 76–90: Close discipline development. Hire is accountable for having a close date and a close plan on every Stage 4 deal. Founder reviews all proposal call recordings. Focus in coaching sessions shifts to close technique and objection handling — the competencies that become relevant once the rep is running full cycles.

**Accountabilities:**
- Quota for Phase 3: 2 closed deals. (Set based on the pipeline built in Phases 1 and 2 — do not set a number that requires pipeline that hasn't been built.)
- Weekly pipeline review submission in advance of coaching session
- Self-score at least 3 calls per week on the scorecard
- Formal win/loss notes on every deal that closes or dies in Phase 3

**How Success Is Measured:**
- 2 closed deals by day 90
- Average call scorecard score of 45+ on recorded calls
- Forecast accuracy: predicted close deals within the phase vs. actual closes, within 30% margin
- Referral attempt made to at least one client who closed in Phase 2 or 3

**Coaching Touchpoints:**
- Weekly: 60-minute pipeline review + deal coaching session. Structured: 30 minutes pipeline review (coaching questions from Section 3), 20 minutes call debrief on one recorded call, 10 minutes rep self-reflection and goal-setting for next week.
- Day 90: comprehensive performance review. Score all 8 competency areas from Section 1. Identify the rep's top 2 strengths (reinforce them) and the 2 lowest scores (build the next 90-day development plan). Celebrate wins — a rep who closed 2 deals in 90 days while learning has done something genuinely difficult.

## SECTION 7: SELF-COACHING PROTOCOL FOR THE FOUNDER

**The fundamental challenge:** The founder is simultaneously the deal-maker, the standard-setter, and the performance evaluator. There is no one to hold the mirror up to them except themselves. This protocol is designed to create that mirror systematically.

---

### Weekly Self-Coaching Routine (30 minutes every Friday)

**What to Review:**

Pull three things: the week's call recordings, the current pipeline with stage dates, and the week's outbound metrics.

**What to Question:**

For each discovery call this week:
- "Did I stay in discovery past 20 minutes, or did I pitch too early?"
- "Can I name the dollar figure we agreed on — and was it their number or mine?"
- "Was the next step I booked specific enough to actually happen?"

For the pipeline:
- "Which deals are older than 10 days in Stage 4, and what is my plan for each one by end of next week — specifically?"
- "Am I carrying any deal in my pipeline primarily because I don't want to admit it's dead?"

For outbound:
- "Which ICP performed best in replies this week, and do I know why?"
- "Did I skip any touches in any sequence this week because I felt uncomfortable — and what does that tell me?"

**What to Change:**

Identify one behavior to do differently next week. Only one. Not three. The goal is consistent incremental improvement, not a complete overhaul that collapses by Wednesday.

Write the behavior down as an observable action: not "be more confident in closing" but "ask the close question at the end of every proposal call, then stay silent for at least 5 seconds."

---

### Monthly Self-Coaching Routine (90 minutes, end of each month)

**What Patterns to Look For in Win/Loss Data:**

Pull every deal that closed (won or lost) in the past 30 days. For each:

1. What was the ICP, and what was the primary pain track that surfaced?
2. What stage did the deal enter and what was the elapsed time in each stage?
3. What was the close probability I assigned at Stage 3 entry, and what was the outcome? (This is your happy-ears calibration number.)
4. If it was a loss: at which stage did the deal actually die, even if it didn't formally exit until later? Most deals die in Stage 2 but aren't declared until Stage 4.
5. What was the documented objection at point of loss? Was it the real objection, or was it the stated reason? (If you don't know the difference, the loss analysis is incomplete.)

**Monthly Questions to Sit With:**

- "Which ICP is producing the most deals — and which is producing the most closed-won deals? Are they the same ICP?"
- "What is my average time from first touch to close — and is it trending up or down?"
- "Am I winning on value or on price? How do I know?"
- "Which failure pattern from Section 5 am I most guilty of this month — and am I improving?"

---

### Quarterly Self-Coaching Routine (Half-day, end of each quarter)

**How to Reset Drifted Habits:**

Sales habits drift. Founders especially. The urgency of early deals ("I'll just say whatever works") calcifies into bad patterns that persist even after the business grows. Every quarter, run a deliberate habit audit.

Step 1: Pull the call scorecard from Section 2 and re-score yourself on three recent calls. Don't argue with the score. Write the number down.

Step 2: Compare your current scorecard average to the prior quarter's. If any competency has drifted down by more than 3 points, that is a habit reset target.

Step 3: Identify the one habit that, if it compounded over the next 12 months, would produce the most revenue impact. This is not always the competency you enjoy working on. It is often the one that feels uncomfortable — which is usually why it drifted.

Step 4: Redesign your next quarter's self-coaching focus. One primary skill target. A specific observable behavior. A measurement method. Nothing else.

**Quarterly Questions:**

- "What has my close rate been for 3 consecutive months, and is it trending in the right direction?"
- "How many referral conversations did I have this quarter — and what was the pipeline result?"
- "Am I still doing discovery the way I know I should, or have I started skipping steps because deals feel faster when I pitch earlier?"
- "What is the one thing I would tell a new rep not to do — and am I doing it myself?"

---

### The One Question to Ask After Every Lost Deal

**"At what exact moment in the process did I know this deal was at risk — and what did I do about it?"**

This question is designed to close the gap between awareness and action. Most deals that are lost were telegraphed early. The founder noticed the signal, rationalized it away, and then was "surprised" at the loss. The honest answer to this question, repeated over a dozen losses, will reveal one or two recurring patterns that are costing the business more than any other single factor. Those patterns are the coaching priority.

## SECTION 8: COACHING CONVERSATION TEMPLATES

---

### Scenario A: Rep Submitted a Proposal 8 Days Ago, No Follow-Up, Prospect Has Gone Quiet

**Hook Question (0:00–0:30):**

"Before we talk about what to do next — tell me what happened between the proposal call and right now. Walk me through every touchpoint since you sent it."

*Why this works:* The rep is expecting to discuss the prospect. This opens with the rep's behavior, not the prospect's silence. It immediately establishes that the conversation is about what the rep did or didn't do, not about the prospect being unresponsive.

**Diagnostic Thread (0:30–2:00):**

After hearing the timeline:

"So in 8 days, you sent the proposal and then [did/didn't] follow up. Help me understand your thinking — what were you hoping would happen in that gap?"

Then: "What specifically did you agree to at the end of the proposal call — was there a date, a specific next step, anything that was confirmed before you hung up?"

If there was no specific next step confirmed: "So the last thing the prospect committed to was... nothing specific. And then you sent a proposal and waited. Walk me through why that felt like the right strategy."

If there was a next step that was broken: "They said they'd come back to you by [date] and they didn't. That's a piece of information. What does a prospect not keeping a commitment they made tell you — and what did you do when they missed it?"

*Why this works:* The diagnostic thread surfaces the two possible failure points: no next step was set at the proposal call, or a next step was set and then allowed to break without consequence. Both are coaching opportunities. The rep identifies the failure themselves, which makes the subsequent corrective action stick.

**Reframe (2:00–3:00):**

"Here's what I want you to sit with: the prospect going quiet is not a decision. It's a status. You don't know whether they're about to say yes, about to say no, or have just gotten overwhelmed this week. What you do know is that every additional day of silence makes it harder, not easier, to re-engage. The 8-day gap is not a problem the prospect created. It's a gap you created by not having a specific next step locked in before that call ended. So the question now is: what are you going to say — specifically, not generally — when you reach out today? Because 'just checking in' is not it."

---

### Scenario B: Rep's Close Rate Has Been Below 25% for 3 Consecutive Months

**Hook Question (0:00–0:30):**

"I want to have a direct conversation about your close rate, and I want to start with a question that I genuinely don't know the answer to yet: when you think about the deals you've lost in the last three months, what's the most common reason they didn't close — in your own words, not the CRM dropdown?"

*Why this works:* The rep is expecting a performance conversation with a verdict. Opening with a genuine question — one the coach doesn't already know the answer to — signals that this is a diagnosis, not a sentencing. It also immediately surfaces whether the rep has been doing honest post-mortems or has been rationalizing losses.

**Diagnostic Thread (0:30–2:00):**

After hearing the rep's answer:

"That's interesting. Now let me ask you something different — if you had to be honest about what you could have done differently in your last three losses, what would it be? Not what the prospect did wrong. What you could have done differently."

Then: "When you say [rep's answer], can you give me a specific example — a specific call, a specific moment where that played out?"

If the rep blames externals (bad timing, price too high, wrong ICP): "I want to push back on that gently. Those might all be true. But a 25% close rate over three months means three out of four deals you bring to proposal stage are not converting. At what point in the process do you think the deal is actually being decided — and are you showing up differently at that moment?"

If the rep identifies a genuine pattern: "Okay — that's a real thing you've identified. What would have to change in the way you're running [that stage of the deal] for that pattern to stop repeating?"

**Reframe (2:00–3:00):**

"Here's what I want you to understand: a 25% close rate doesn't mean you're having bad luck three months in a row. It means something in the process is producing a predictable result — and predictable results have a predictable cause. We're going to find it. It might be in discovery, it might be in the proposal call, it might be in close discipline — but it's in there somewhere, and the fact that it's been consistent for three months means it's a pattern, not noise. Patterns are fixable. What I want to do for the next 20 minutes is pick apart the last three losses together, not to assign blame, but to find the one thing that, if you changed it today, would change the number. Are you open to that?"

---

### Scenario C: Rep Is Excellent at Discovery but Consistently Fails to Ask for the Decision at the End of Proposal Calls

**Hook Question (0:00–0:30):**

"You are genuinely one of the best discovery interviewers I've seen — and I mean that. I want to ask you something that might feel strange: why do you think it's easier for you to ask hard questions at the beginning of a deal than at the end of one?"

*Why this works:* This opens with a real compliment that is earned, not performative, and then immediately creates cognitive dissonance. The rep knows they're good at discovery. The question makes them sit with the paradox of being good at asking questions in one context and avoidant in another. It sets up the conversation as curious rather than critical.

**Diagnostic Thread (0:30–2:00):**

After hearing their answer (which will usually involve some version of "I don't want to be pushy" or "I want them to feel like it's their decision"):

"Tell me what you think happens in the prospect's head when they get to the end of a proposal call and the rep doesn't ask for a decision. Walk me through what they're likely thinking."

Then: "Now tell me what happens to that prospect in the 48 hours after the call when there's no specific next step — what does their world look like?"

If the rep says "they'll review it and probably get back to me": "What percentage of your proposal calls that ended without a close question have actually converted to a closed deal — do you know that number?"

If they don't know: "I want you to pull that number this week, because I think when you see it, it's going to reframe what 'not being pushy' is actually costing you. Here's the question I want you to sit with: is it possible that what feels like respect for the prospect's process is actually the thing that's making their decision harder, not easier?"

**Reframe (2:00–3:00):**

"You've built a real skill in discovery — you know how to make a prospect feel understood, how to uncover pain they didn't realize they were going to share. That is genuinely hard. What I want you to see is that asking for the decision at the end of a proposal call is not a different skill. It's the same skill. Discovery is asking a question and holding space for an honest answer. Closing is asking a question and holding space for an honest answer. The only difference is what the question is. 'Are you ready to move forward?' is just discovery — it's asking the prospect to tell you where they really are. When you don't ask it, you're making the decision for them by default. You're choosing 'not yet' on their behalf without asking. The prospect who's on the fence and gets asked directly often makes a decision they're actually ready to make. The one who doesn't get asked goes home and gets distracted. You're not being pushy by asking. You're finishing what you started. Let's spend 15 minutes building a close question that sounds like you — not like a sales script — and then we'll role-play it until it feels natural."

## FINAL NOTE: THE COACHING COMPACT

This system only works if the following principles hold:

**On observation:** You cannot coach what you haven't witnessed. Listen to at least one recorded call per rep per week. There is no shortcut to this.

**On specificity:** Every coaching conversation must be anchored to a specific observable behavior on a specific call or in a specific deal. "You need to be more confident" is not coaching. "On the Friction Audit call with [prospect] on Tuesday, you started talking about the solution at minute 8 — let's talk about what happened there" is coaching.

**On frequency:** Coaching once a month is motivation. Coaching once a week is development. A rep who gets 2 hours of coaching a week will outperform a rep who gets 30 minutes by 13 percentage points in win rate. The investment compounds.

**On the will vs. skill distinction:** If a rep knows what good looks like, has been shown what good looks like, and still doesn't do it — that is a will problem. Will problems are managed through management (clear expectations, clear consequences). Skill problems are solved through coaching (observation, question, practice, repetition). Never coach a will problem as if it were a skill problem. You will exhaust yourself and enable the rep.

**On self-compassion for the founder:** You are coaching a team while running a business and selling deals yourself. You will not execute this system perfectly every week. A partial coaching system — run consistently — beats a perfect one that collapses under operational pressure. Pick the one section of this system that produces the most impact for your current stage and run it religiously. Add the next section when that one is working.

The goal is not to produce perfect reps. The goal is to produce a learning system where every deal — won or lost — makes the whole team better.
